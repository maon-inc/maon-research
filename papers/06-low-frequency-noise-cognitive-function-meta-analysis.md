## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis examining how low-frequency noise exposure affects cognitive function across four domains: attention, executive function, memory, and higher-order cognitive functions. Eight relevant studies were analyzed, revealing that low-frequency noise selectively impairs higher-order cognitive tasks such as logical reasoning, mathematical calculation, and data processing, while simpler cognitive functions showed more resilience.

## Key Findings

- Low-frequency noise had a statistically significant negative impact on higher-order cognitive functions (Z = 2.42, p = 0.02)
- Standardized mean difference for higher-order cognition was -0.37 (95% CI: -0.67 to -0.07)
- Moderate heterogeneity was observed among studies (I-squared = 29%)
- Attention, executive function, and memory were not significantly impaired by low-frequency noise alone
- The selective vulnerability of complex cognitive tasks suggests noise disrupts the integration of multiple cognitive processes
- Environmental noise at work and home settings was the primary exposure context studied

## MAON Applicability

MAON tracks both headphone noise and environmental noise exposure through the baseline engine. This meta-analysis suggests that MAON should pay particular attention to sustained low-frequency noise environments, as they may impair complex cognitive tasks without the user noticing degradation in simpler activities. The cognitive score feature, which integrates health, screen time, calendar, and location signals, could incorporate noise exposure as a modulating factor. When environmental noise is elevated, MAON could flag that cognitive performance on demanding tasks may be reduced.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines with meta-analysis of 8 studies. Cognitive outcomes categorized into four domains. Random-effects model used due to heterogeneity. Limited by the small number of included studies and varied noise exposure parameters across studies.
