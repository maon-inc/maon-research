## Summary

A meta-analysis of 10 RCTs (N=1,491) examining whether reducing social media use decreases depression symptoms. Finds a significant small-to-medium effect size, providing causal evidence for the social media-depression link.

## Key Findings

- Reducing social media use significantly decreases depressive symptoms (g = 0.25)
- Meta-analysis of 10 RCTs with 1,491 total participants
- Effect observed across different reduction strategies (complete abstinence, time limits)
- One-week social media breaks produce significant improvements in depression and anxiety
- Limiting to 1 hour/day reduces depression symptoms by 2.36 points and anxiety by 2.35 points

## MAON Applicability

Provides evidence that MAON's screen time nudges could have therapeutic value. If MAON detects escalating social media use alongside worsening mood indicators, suggesting social media reduction is evidence-based. Quantifies expected effect sizes for such interventions.

## Methodology Notes

Meta-analysis of RCTs (highest evidence quality) published in EJIHPE (2024). 10 trials, 1,491 participants. Various reduction strategies tested. Open access.
