## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis examines the relationship between light at night (LAN) exposure and mental health outcomes across 19 studies with a combined population of 556,861 participants. The analysis finds modest but consistent associations between greater nighttime light exposure and worse mental health, with particular emphasis on depressive symptoms and bipolar disorder prevalence.

## Key Findings

- Included 19 studies with a total population of 556,861 participants
- Greater light at night exposure was associated with increased depressive symptomatology across studies
- A higher prevalence of bipolar disorder was linked to LAN exposure
- Indoor artificial light at night showed a stronger effect than outdoor light (a 1 lux increase in indoor LAN was associated with a 3.29% increase in depression risk)
- Outdoor artificial light at night showed a smaller but significant effect (a 1 nW/cm2/sr increase associated with a 0.43% increase in depression risk)
- The association was consistent across multiple measurement methods for light exposure
- Results support circadian disruption as a mechanistic pathway linking LAN to mood disorders

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's circadian rhythm and sleep tracking features. While MAON does not directly measure light exposure, the findings support the importance of sleep timing and circadian regularity metrics that MAON does track. The association between LAN and mood disorders strengthens the rationale for MAON's proactive nudges around sleep hygiene and consistent sleep-wake schedules. Could inform future feature development around light exposure recommendations.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following standard guidelines. Included observational studies measuring light at night exposure (via satellite data, personal dosimeters, or self-report) and mental health outcomes (clinical diagnoses or validated questionnaires). The 19 included studies varied in measurement methods, which contributes to heterogeneity. The large aggregate sample size (556,861) provides good statistical power for population-level associations.
