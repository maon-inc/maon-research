## Summary

A systematic review evaluating whether objective data from wearable devices and smartphones can longitudinally monitor and predict changes in depression severity within diagnosed individuals. The review assesses the ability of mobile sensing to distinguish different depressive states, predict symptom trajectory, and identify clinically relevant objective features for tracking depression over time.

## Key Findings

- Four studies reporting predictive accuracy for depression achieved 81% to 91% accuracy, with higher accuracy from personalized models or multimodal data
- Personalized models consistently outperformed group-level models for predicting individual depression trajectories
- Multimodal data fusion (combining multiple sensor streams) improved prediction accuracy over single-sensor approaches
- Heart rate, step count, and sleep patterns emerged as the most commonly used and effective features for depression monitoring
- GPS-based mobility features and social interaction patterns provided complementary predictive value
- Most studies used monitoring periods of less than 7 days, highlighting the need for longer longitudinal assessments
- Clinical translation requires standardized protocols, larger sample sizes, and longitudinal studies of 3+ months

Relevant to MAON's overall approach of combining multiple passive sensing streams (Apple Health biometrics, screen time, location, calendar) into a composite wellness assessment. The finding that multimodal data and personalized models yield 81-91% accuracy for depression monitoring supports MAON's multi-signal cognitive score architecture. ## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Focused specifically on studies involving individuals with diagnosed depression (not subclinical populations). Evaluated three research questions: state monitoring, severity prediction, and feature identification. Authors affiliated with Goethe University Frankfurt, German Foundation for Depression and Suicide Prevention, and University of Oslo.
