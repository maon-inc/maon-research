## Summary

A review article examining how depression alters sleep architecture, covering changes in REM sleep, slow wave sleep, and sleep continuity. Provides a framework for understanding sleep-mood bidirectional relationships.

## Key Findings

- Depression is characterized by shortened REM latency, increased REM density, and increased total REM time
- Slow wave sleep (deep sleep) may be reduced in depression, though findings are less consistent than REM changes
- Sleep fragmentation and reduced sleep efficiency are robust markers across mood disorders
- Bidirectional relationship exists: poor sleep architecture worsens depression, and depression disrupts sleep

## MAON Applicability

Reinforces MAON's sleep quality scoring approach. The bidirectional relationship means MAON should treat deteriorating sleep architecture as both a symptom and a risk factor for mood episodes, warranting proactive outreach.

## Methodology Notes

Review article published in PMC (2025). Synthesizes current understanding of sleep-depression relationships. Covers both clinical and subclinical populations.
