## Summary

This large prospective cohort study of 73,411 adults (mean age 56.08 years; 55.72% women) investigated the relationship between accelerometer-measured physical activity, sedentary behavior, and incident neuropsychiatric diseases. The study found that moderate-to-vigorous physical activity showed the strongest protective effect against neuropsychiatric diseases, with hazard ratios of 0.60 to 0.86.

## Key Findings

- Moderate-to-vigorous physical activity energy expenditure showed the strongest protective effect on neuropsychiatric diseases (hazard ratios: 0.60-0.86, all FDR-Q < 0.001).
- Higher levels of objectively measured physical activity were associated with reduced risk of depression, anxiety, and other neuropsychiatric conditions.
- Sedentary behavior was associated with increased neuropsychiatric disease incidence in a dose-dependent manner.
- The use of accelerometer-based measurement (rather than self-report) provided more precise estimates of the activity-disease relationship.
- The large sample size (n=73,411) and prospective design strengthen the causal inference compared to cross-sectional studies.
- The study controlled for multiple confounders and used false discovery rate correction for multiple comparisons, enhancing statistical rigor.

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's approach of using wearable accelerometer data (via Apple Health and Garmin) to monitor physical activity and infer mental health status. The protective hazard ratios for moderate-to-vigorous activity support MAON's cognitive score health domain, which incorporates step counts and activity data. The neuropsychiatric disease outcomes (depression, anxiety) align directly with MAON's target conditions. The study's use of consumer-grade accelerometry validates the general approach of wearable-based activity monitoring for mental health prediction.

## Methodology Notes

Prospective cohort study with 73,411 participants (mean age 56.08 years, 55.72% women, 96.4% white). Physical activity and sedentary behavior were measured objectively using wrist-worn accelerometers. Follow-up tracked incident neuropsychiatric diagnoses. Statistical analysis used Cox proportional hazards models with FDR correction. Published in Molecular Psychiatry (2025/2026), volume 31, pages 1485-1499.
