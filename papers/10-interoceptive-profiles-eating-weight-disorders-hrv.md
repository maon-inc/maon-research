## Summary

This study examined electrocardiogram and electroencephalogram recordings from adult women with anorexia nervosa (n=38), bulimia nervosa (n=35), binge eating disorder (n=22), obesity (n=20), and healthy controls (n=46) during resting state and a heartbeat counting task. Results reveal disorder-specific interoceptive profiles rather than a generalized interoceptive deficit across eating and weight disorders.

## Key Findings

- Patients with anorexia nervosa, bulimia nervosa, and obesity all showed reduced vagally-mediated heart rate variability at the peripheral level
- Only patients with anorexia nervosa showed increased central processing of heartbeats (heartbeat-evoked potentials), suggesting heightened cardiac interoception
- The results do not support the notion of general interoceptive deficits; instead, each disorder has a distinct interoceptive profile
- Binge eating disorder showed peripheral autonomic changes without central interoceptive alterations
- Obesity was associated with reduced HRV but normal central heartbeat processing
- These disorder-specific patterns may inform targeted treatment approaches that address the particular interoceptive dysfunction in each condition

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's eating disorder monitoring features (Domain 10) and HRV-based health assessment (Domain 1). The finding that different eating and weight disorders show distinct HRV patterns supports MAON's use of wearable-derived HRV as a potential marker for eating disorder severity and subtype differentiation. The reduced vagal tone findings across multiple conditions validate MAON's approach of tracking HRV as a transdiagnostic autonomic indicator, while the disorder-specific profiles suggest that MAON could refine its algorithms to distinguish between different eating pathologies based on combined HRV and behavioral data.

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional study with 161 participants across five groups. Used clinical-grade ECG and EEG for both peripheral (HRV) and central (heartbeat-evoked potentials) interoceptive measurement. The multi-group design with clearly defined diagnostic criteria strengthens generalizability. Limitation is the cross-sectional design, which prevents causal inference about whether interoceptive changes precede or follow disorder onset.
