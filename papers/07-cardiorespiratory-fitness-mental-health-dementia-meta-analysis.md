## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 27 studies encompassing over 4 million individuals examined the relationship between cardiorespiratory fitness (CRF) and the incidence of mental disorders and dementia. High CRF was associated with substantially lower risk of depression (HR 0.64), psychotic disorders (HR 0.71), and all-cause dementia (HR 0.61) compared to low CRF. Each 1 MET increase in fitness was associated with a 5% reduction in depression risk, making CRF a powerful modifiable protective factor.

## Key Findings

- High CRF was associated with a 36% reduced hazard of incident depression compared to low CRF (HR 0.64, 95% CI 0.56-0.74)
- High CRF reduced risk of psychotic disorders by 29% (HR 0.71) and all-cause dementia by 39% (HR 0.61)
- Each 1 MET increase in CRF was associated with a 5% lower depression risk (HR 0.95, 95% CI 0.92-0.98)
- Effects were consistent across the lifespan, from adolescents to older adults
- The dose-response relationship supports targeting CRF improvements through exercise as a mental health strategy
- 27 studies, 4,007,638 participants: the largest meta-analysis of CRF and mental health to date

## MAON Applicability

MAON tracks VO2 max via Apple Health (DAILY tier). VO2 max is the gold-standard CRF measure. This meta-analysis in Nature Mental Health provides the strongest population-level evidence that MAON should surface VO2 max trends as a mental health biomarker. Declines in VO2 max could be flagged as a mental health risk indicator; improvements could be positively reinforced through the AI coaching interface.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis. 27 prospective cohort studies. N=4,007,638. Hazard ratios computed from adjusted models in original studies. Published 2026 in Nature Mental Health. Covers CRF across general populations, not clinical samples. Represents the most comprehensive and statistically powered evidence on this relationship to date.
