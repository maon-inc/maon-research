## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis examined 29 interventional studies (13 RCTs) on chatbot-delivered mental health interventions for young people, covering publications from January 2014 to September 2024 across 11 databases. The meta-analysis found that chatbot interventions significantly reduced psychological distress (Hedge's g = -0.28) but did not significantly affect psychological well-being, with treatment effects moderated by sample type, delivery platform, interaction mode, and response generation approach.

## Key Findings

- Chatbot-delivered interventions significantly reduced distress among young people (Hedge's g = -0.28, 95% CI [-0.46, -0.10])
- No significant effect was found on psychological well-being (Hedge's g = 0.13, 95% CI [-0.16, 0.41])
- Treatment effects were moderated by sample type, delivery platform, interaction mode, and response generation approach
- Rule-based systems dominated until 2023, but LLM-based chatbots surged to 45% of new studies in 2024
- Only 16% of LLM-based chatbot studies had undergone clinical efficacy testing, with most (77%) still in early validation
- Chatbot interventions were found to be feasible and acceptable, with potential to supplement existing mental health services

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's chat feature and proactive agent, which functions as an AI-powered conversational wellness companion. The meta-analytic evidence that chatbots reduce distress supports the potential effectiveness of MAON's approach. The finding that delivery platform and interaction mode moderate treatment effects is important for MAON's design decisions around push notifications, chat interface, and response style. The distinction between distress reduction and well-being promotion suggests MAON should track both outcome types when evaluating its impact on users.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines with meta-analysis. Searched 11 databases covering January 2014 to September 2024. Included 29 interventional studies (13 RCTs). Effect sizes calculated using Hedge's g. Moderator analyses examined sample characteristics, platform features, and chatbot design. Published in Worldviews on Evidence-Based Nursing, a peer-reviewed nursing research journal.
