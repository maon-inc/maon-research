## Summary

This systematic review evaluated smartphone apps across a wide range of mental health disorders in terms of clinical effectiveness, feasibility, and acceptability. From 4,153 non-duplicate articles screened, 31 RCTs met eligibility criteria, including 6 treatment apps, 4 self-monitoring apps, and 21 multipurpose apps. The authors propose five recommendations for improving clinical translation of mental health apps.

## Key Findings

- Four studies demonstrated statistically significant effects, reducing symptoms of acrophobia, depression, and anxiety, with several also reporting improvements in quality-of-life metrics
- Self-monitoring apps showed promise as first-step interventions, helping users gain information about their mental health and utilize more adaptive coping strategies
- While smartphone apps were generally effective and acceptable, feasibility appeared to decline over time, suggesting engagement is a key challenge
- Treatment apps that incorporated CBT techniques showed the strongest evidence base
- Multipurpose apps combining monitoring, psychoeducation, and active intervention elements were the most common design pattern
- The review identified underrepresented demographics and potential biases as areas requiring further research

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON as a smartphone-based wellness app that combines self-monitoring (via wearable data), AI-generated insights, and active interventions (breathing exercises, calendar blocking). The finding that feasibility declines over time validates MAON's approach of using proactive AI engagement and personalized insights to maintain user engagement. The five recommendations for clinical translation can guide MAON's development priorities.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Searched multiple databases for RCTs comparing mental health apps with treatment-as-usual or no treatment in clinical populations. Excluded web-based interventions and physical health apps. 31 studies met full-text eligibility from 4,153 screened. Authors from King's College London. Published in BMJ Open, February 2025.
