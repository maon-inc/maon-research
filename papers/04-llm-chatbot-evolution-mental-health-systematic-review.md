## Summary

A systematic review of 160 studies (2020-2024) classifying AI mental health chatbot architectures into rule-based, machine-learning-based, and LLM-based systems. The review proposes a three-tier evaluation framework (bench testing, pilot feasibility, clinical efficacy) and tracks the rapid shift toward LLM-based systems. Despite explosive growth, most LLM chatbots remain in early validation phases with limited clinical efficacy evidence.

## Key Findings

- LLM-based chatbots surged from near-zero to 45% of new mental health chatbot studies in 2024 alone
- Only 16% of LLM-based chatbot studies underwent clinical efficacy testing; 77% remained in bench or pilot testing phases
- Rule-based systems dominated through 2023 but have given way to generative AI approaches
- Three evaluation tiers identified: foundational bench testing (technical validation), pilot feasibility (user engagement), and clinical efficacy (symptom reduction)
- Anxiety and depression are the most commonly targeted conditions in chatbot research
- Safety, hallucination, and boundary-setting remain underaddressed in LLM chatbot deployments

## MAON Applicability

MAON's iMessage AI coaching interface (Kimi K2.5) is a real-world deployment of the LLM-based chatbot paradigm this review describes. The three-tier evaluation framework provides a roadmap for how MAON should be tested and validated as it scales. The finding that clinical efficacy evidence lags technical deployment directly motivates MAON's research-backed design philosophy.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review of 160 empirical studies published 2020-2024. Coverage across PubMed, PsycINFO, and grey literature. Authors include researchers from Harvard Medical School (Torous J), Boston Children's Hospital, and multiple international institutions. Published October 2025 in World Psychiatry, volume 24, pages 383-394.
