## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 9 RCTs involving 3,703 adolescent participants evaluating the effectiveness of mHealth interventions that target health behaviors (physical activity, sedentary behavior, sleep, or nutrition) on emotional, behavioral, and eating disorders. Found that mHealth interventions may effectively reduce anxiety and eating disorder symptoms, with more modest and mixed effects on depression and behavioral disorders.

## Key Findings

- mHealth behavior change interventions showed effectiveness in reducing anxiety symptoms in adolescents
- Eating disorder symptoms were also reduced by interventions targeting physical activity, nutrition, and sedentary behavior
- Effects on depression and behavioral disorders were modest and inconsistent across studies
- Mobile health technologies including smartphone apps, wearables, and SMS showed promise as delivery mechanisms for adolescent behavioral interventions
- Considerable heterogeneity across included studies limits the strength of conclusions
- Low certainty of evidence underscores the need for further high-quality RCTs with longer follow-up periods

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's role as a wellness companion that integrates multiple health behavior streams (activity, sleep, screen time). This meta-analysis supports the approach of using mobile and wearable technology to deliver behavioral interventions, particularly for younger populations. MAON's combination of activity tracking, sleep monitoring, screen time management, and proactive messaging aligns with the multimodal mHealth approach validated here. The finding that interventions targeting multiple health behaviors can reduce eating disorder and anxiety symptoms is particularly relevant for MAON's holistic health monitoring strategy.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis of 9 randomized controlled trials with 3,703 adolescent participants total. Published in Frontiers in Digital Health (2025). Searches completed through September 2024. Assessed risk of bias and certainty of evidence using GRADE methodology.
