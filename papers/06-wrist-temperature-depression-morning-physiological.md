## Summary

This study used a wearable thermopile sensor to measure peripheral wrist skin temperature and heart rate, comparing morning physiological characteristics between patients with major depressive disorder (MDD) and healthy controls. The research found that MDD patients exhibited significantly lower wrist skin temperature at 09:00 compared to healthy participants, providing evidence for circadian-linked physiological disruption in depression.

## Key Findings

- Patients with MDD showed approximately 2.9 degrees C lower peripheral wrist skin temperature at 09:00 compared to healthy controls.
- The finding aligns with the clinical observation that many MDD patients feel worse in the morning, linking subjective experience to measurable physiological disruption.
- Wearable wrist-worn temperature sensors can detect clinically meaningful differences in circadian temperature patterns.
- The morning temperature dip in MDD patients suggests disrupted circadian thermoregulation as a potential biomarker for depression severity.
- Combined temperature and heart rate monitoring via wearables provides complementary physiological signals for mood disorder detection.
- Consumer-grade wrist-worn devices could potentially screen for depression-related circadian disruption through continuous temperature monitoring.

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's Apple Health integration and physiological monitoring capabilities. While MAON currently tracks HRV and resting heart rate, wrist skin temperature data from Apple Watch could be incorporated as an additional circadian health signal. The finding that morning physiological measurements are most discriminative for depression supports time-of-day-aware analysis in MAON's summary and trend features. This could inform the cognitive score's health domain by adding temperature-based circadian markers.

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional comparison study using wearable thermopile sensors on MDD patients and healthy controls. Measured peripheral wrist skin temperature and heart rate with focus on morning (09:00) measurements. Published 2025.
