## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 19 studies (n=2,930) examining the relationship between passively collected GPS mobility metrics from smartphones and depressive symptom severity. The study provides the first pooled effect size estimates for specific GPS features as digital biomarkers of depression, finding small but significant between-person correlations for multiple mobility metrics.

## Key Findings

- Distance traveled showed the strongest correlation with depression (r=-0.25, 95% CI -0.29 to -0.21), with greater depression linked to less movement
- Normalized entropy (r=-0.17) and location variance (r=-0.17) were significantly correlated, indicating depressed individuals visit fewer unique locations
- Entropy (r=-0.13) and number of location clusters (r=-0.11) showed smaller but significant associations
- Homestay showed a positive correlation (r=0.10), meaning more time spent at home was associated with greater depressive symptoms
- Within-person correlations were too heterogeneous across studies to meta-analyze, highlighting a key research gap
- Study quality was generally low, with no studies fully adhering to STROBE reporting guidelines

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's location tracking feature, which monitors visit patterns and location routines as part of the cognitive score. The finding that reduced mobility, fewer visited locations, and increased homestay correlate with depression supports MAON's approach of scoring location-based routine as a wellness signal. The specific GPS metrics identified (distance, entropy, location variance, homestay) can inform how MAON weights and interprets location data.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines with meta-analysis. Searched multiple databases for studies published through 2023. Included 19 studies with 2,930 participants (mean age 38.42, 59.64% female). Pooled between-person correlations using random-effects models. Quality assessed using STROBE checklist.
