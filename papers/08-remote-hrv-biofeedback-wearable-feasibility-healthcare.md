## Summary

A prospective feasibility study of 127 healthcare workers across 7 New York City hospitals who used a smartphone-based HRV biofeedback program for 5 minutes daily over 5 weeks while wearing an Apple Watch to monitor circadian HRV features. The study found that compliance was very low (16.5% achieved at least 50% adherence), raising important questions about the feasibility of remote, self-directed breathing interventions without additional support structures.

## Key Findings

- Only 21 of 127 participants (16.5%) achieved at least 50% compliance with daily 5-minute HRV biofeedback sessions
- Among compliant participants, a mixed-effect cosinor model showed increased MESOR of circadian SDNN over the first 4 weeks of biofeedback
- Numerical improvements in psychological metrics (stress, anxiety) were observed but did not reach statistical significance
- The Apple Watch successfully captured circadian HRV patterns for remote monitoring of the intervention's physiological effects
- Low compliance highlights the challenge of sustaining self-directed breathing practice even with brief sessions
- The study suggests that additional engagement strategies, reminders, or gamification may be needed to improve adherence

## MAON Applicability

Critically relevant to MAON's breathing activity feature. The low compliance rate (16.5%) is a cautionary finding: even well-motivated healthcare workers struggled to maintain a 5-minute daily breathing practice. This supports MAON's approach of using proactive AI-triggered interventions (via the sendblue agent) rather than relying on user-initiated practice. MAON could leverage Apple Watch HRV data to verify whether breathing exercises are producing measurable autonomic changes, similar to this study's SDNN monitoring approach.

## Methodology Notes

Open-label prospective feasibility study with 127 healthcare workers from 7 NYC hospitals. Participants used a smartphone HRV biofeedback app for 5 min/day for 5 weeks, then were followed for 12 additional weeks. Apple Watch collected circadian HRV data throughout. Published in JMIR Mental Health (2024). The study provides important real-world compliance data for remote wearable-monitored breathing interventions.
