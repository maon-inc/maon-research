## Summary

A prospective cohort study in The Lancet Psychiatry tracking objectively-measured physical activity and sedentary behavior throughout adolescence, examining their associations with depressive symptoms. Finds that both reduced activity and increased sedentary time predict depression.

## Key Findings

- Accelerometer-measured sedentary behavior throughout adolescence predicts depressive symptoms
- Each additional 60 minutes of daily sedentary behavior at age 12 associated with increased depression at age 18
- Light activity replacement of sedentary time associated with lower depression scores
- Both light and moderate-vigorous activity had protective effects
- Longitudinal trajectory matters: declining activity over adolescence is a risk signal

## MAON Applicability

Supports MAON monitoring activity trajectories over time, not just current levels. A declining step count trend over weeks could be an early depression indicator, particularly in younger users. The light activity finding suggests even modest activity increases are meaningful.

## Methodology Notes

Prospective cohort published in The Lancet Psychiatry (2020). Adolescent sample with longitudinal follow-up. Accelerometer-measured activity. High-impact journal.
