## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 25 randomized controlled trials examining the moderate- to long-term effects (3+ months) of dietary interventions on depression and anxiety symptoms. Calorie restriction dietary advice showed a small but significant improvement in depressive symptoms among adults with elevated cardiometabolic risk. Evidence for Mediterranean diet and other dietary patterns on depression, and all dietary effects on anxiety, remained limited.

## Key Findings

- Calorie restriction dietary advice improved depressive symptoms (SMD: -0.23, 95% CI: -0.38 to -0.09) in adults with elevated cardiometabolic risk
- The evidence was rated low certainty due to study heterogeneity and risk of bias
- Mediterranean diet showed promising but inconclusive effects on depression, with limited trial data
- Effects of dietary interventions on anxiety symptoms were insufficient for firm conclusions
- Most benefit was observed in populations with existing cardiometabolic risk factors
- Dietary interventions were compared against no dietary advice or active control interventions over 3+ months

## MAON Applicability

MAON's baseline engine tracks dietary macronutrients (energy, protein, carbs, fat, caffeine, water) with 14-day rolling windows, and body composition metrics (weight, BMI, body fat) with 90-day windows. This meta-analysis provides evidence that dietary patterns can meaningfully influence depressive symptoms, especially in cardiometabolic-risk populations. MAON could surface insights connecting dietary trends with mood indicators from other signals (HRV, sleep, activity). The finding that calorie restriction helps depression in cardiometabolic-risk users could inform personalized nudges when MAON detects both elevated body composition metrics and declining mood indicators.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis of 25 RCTs following PRISMA guidelines, published in Annals of Internal Medicine. Inclusion criteria: dietary advice with or without food provision, 3+ month follow-up, depression and/or anxiety outcomes. GRADE certainty of evidence assessment applied. Low certainty for most comparisons due to heterogeneity and risk of bias limitations.
