## Summary

This large-scale systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis synthesized evidence from 57 studies across 35 cohorts (31 studies in meta-analyses) to determine the dose-response relationship between daily step count and multiple health outcomes including depression, mortality, cardiovascular disease, cancer, diabetes, dementia, and falls. The study found that approximately 7,000 daily steps is a practical threshold associated with clinically meaningful benefits across outcomes, including a 22% reduction in depression risk.

## Key Findings

- Walking approximately 7,000 steps per day was associated with a 22% reduction in depression risk, along with reductions in all-cause mortality (47%), cardiovascular disease (25%), dementia (38%), type 2 diabetes (14%), cancer (6%), and falls (28%).
- The dose-response relationship between steps and depression showed an inverse linear association, meaning incremental step increases yield proportional mental health benefits.
- While 10,000 steps per day remains a viable target for more active individuals, 7,000 steps per day provides clinically meaningful improvements and may be a more realistic and achievable target for many populations.
- The study synthesized prospective dose-response data from literature published between January 2014 and February 2025, providing the most comprehensive step-count meta-analysis to date.
- Step count measured by consumer wearables and accelerometers provides an objective, scalable marker of physical activity that can be passively monitored in real-world settings.
- Benefits accrued in a graded fashion, with even modest increases (e.g., 1,000 extra steps per day) conferring measurable health improvements.

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's cognitive score health domain, which uses Apple Health step data with a default target of 10,000 steps. This paper supports validating MAON's step-based scoring approach and could inform the threshold used in the scoring algorithm. The 7,000-step threshold finding may help MAON personalize step targets or provide contextual messaging to users about meaningful activity levels. The depression-specific dose-response curve is especially relevant given MAON's focus on mental health monitoring.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis covering 57 studies from 35 cohorts. Literature searched from PubMed, EBSCO CINAHL, and supplementary sources from January 2014 to February 2025. Meta-analyses included 31 studies from 24 cohorts. High methodological rigor with prospective cohort designs and objective step measurement via accelerometers and pedometers.
