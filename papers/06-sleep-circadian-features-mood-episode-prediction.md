## Summary

Uses mathematical modeling on longitudinal wearable data from 168 mood disorder patients to derive 36 sleep and circadian rhythm features for next-day mood episode prediction. Circadian phase shifts are the most significant predictors.

## Key Findings

- 36 sleep and circadian features derived from wearable data enable accurate next-day mood episode prediction
- Daily circadian phase shifts are the most significant predictors of mood episodes
- Phase delays linked to depressive episodes; phase advances linked to manic episodes
- Longitudinal wearable data from 168 patients provides robust individual-level models
- Prediction accuracy sufficient for clinical early warning systems

## MAON Applicability

Highest-value paper for MAON's bipolar user population. The specific finding that circadian phase delays predict depression and phase advances predict mania gives MAON actionable detection rules. MAON can compute circadian phase from sleep timing data.
