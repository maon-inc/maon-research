## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 29 randomized controlled trials (n=9,475) evaluating fully automated digital cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (FA dCBT-I). The study found moderate to large effects on insomnia severity compared to control groups, though FA dCBT-I was less effective than therapist-assisted CBT-I. Notably, treatment adherence rather than mere completion was identified as the key factor driving effectiveness.

## Key Findings

- 29 RCTs with 9,475 participants were included in the meta-analysis
- FA dCBT-I showed moderate to large effect sizes on insomnia severity versus control groups
- FA dCBT-I was less effective than therapist-assisted CBT-I, indicating a role for human or AI-mediated support
- Control group type significantly moderated outcomes in meta-regression analysis
- Completion rate alone did not moderate outcomes; treatment adherence quality mattered more
- Fully automated delivery enables scalability without human therapist involvement
- The study was registered with PROSPERO (CRD42024526617) and followed PRISMA reporting guidelines

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's sleep tracking and intervention capabilities. The finding that fully automated digital interventions can achieve moderate to large effects on insomnia validates MAON's approach of delivering automated wellness interventions without human therapist involvement. The critical role of treatment adherence (not just completion) supports MAON's proactive agent strategy, which monitors engagement patterns and adapts outreach timing. MAON's calm interventions with breathing exercises align with CBT-I relaxation components. ## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Registered with PROSPERO. Searched multiple databases for RCTs comparing fully automated (no human therapist involvement) digital CBT-I against control conditions. Subgroup analyses examined control group type, intervention duration, and population characteristics. Meta-regression tested moderators including completion rate. Risk of bias assessed using Cochrane tools.
