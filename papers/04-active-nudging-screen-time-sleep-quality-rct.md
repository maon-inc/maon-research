## Summary

An exploratory randomized controlled trial (N=17) comparing a tracking-only condition with an active digital nudge condition over 7 days. While no significant overall screen time reduction was found as a primary outcome, the study demonstrated a direct correlation between screen time reduction and improved sleep quality, and found a significant effect of reduced frequency of sleep delay in the active nudge condition.

## Key Findings

- Active digital nudges did not produce a statistically significant overall reduction in screen time as a primary outcome, suggesting that nudges alone may not be sufficient for heavy users
- A direct correlation was identified between screen time reduction and improved sleep quality, reinforcing the link between digital behavior and sleep health
- The active nudge condition significantly reduced the frequency of sleep delay (p=0.026), suggesting targeted benefits for bedtime-related phone use
- The study highlights that even modest reductions in screen time, when they occur near bedtime, can yield meaningful improvements in sleep outcomes
- Tracking alone (passive monitoring) showed minimal behavior change, suggesting that active intervention is needed beyond simple awareness
- The findings support combining nudge-based interventions with more structured blocking or restriction approaches for greater effectiveness

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's smart schedule feature and its screen time monitoring capabilities. The finding that screen time reduction correlates with improved sleep quality supports MAON's approach of integrating screen time data with sleep and wellness metrics in its cognitive score. The sleep delay finding specifically validates the concept of time-based app blocking (as MAON's smart schedule implements) rather than relying solely on nudges. MAON could consider a layered approach: nudges for moderate users and automated blocking for those with persistent patterns.

## Methodology Notes

Exploratory randomized controlled trial with N=17 participants over a 7-day period. Two conditions: tracking-only (control) vs. active digital nudge (intervention). Outcomes included objectively measured screen time and subjective sleep quality. Small sample size limits generalizability, but the significant sleep delay finding provides a meaningful signal. Published in Frontiers in Psychiatry (2025).
