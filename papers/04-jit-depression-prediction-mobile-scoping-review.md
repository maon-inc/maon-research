## Summary

This scoping review published in Nature Mental Health synthesized findings from 52 studies on mobile technology for just-in-time prediction of depression. The review identified commonly used passive sensing features, evaluated their predictive value, and assessed methodological approaches. Key features included location data, sleep metrics, physical activity, communication patterns, heart rate variability, and mood self-reports. Personalized models and anomaly detection consistently outperformed generalized approaches.

## Key Findings

- Features most strongly associated with depressive symptoms included time spent at home, sleep variability, and reduced mobility, all of which can be captured by consumer wearables and smartphones.
- Combining physiological, behavioral, and self-report data enhanced predictive performance compared to any single data stream alone.
- Personalized models substantially outperformed generalized population-level models in just-in-time depression prediction.
- Anomaly detection approaches (identifying deviations from individual baselines) showed particular promise for predicting symptom changes.
- Heart rate variability, location data, and sleep metrics were among the most frequently assessed passive sensing features across the 52 included studies.
- The review identified methodological gaps including small sample sizes, lack of diverse populations, and limited real-world deployment evidence.
