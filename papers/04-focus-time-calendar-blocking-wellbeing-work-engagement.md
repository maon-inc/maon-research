## Summary

An experimental study evaluating the effectiveness of automatically scheduled focus time on Outlook calendars for improving wellbeing and work engagement among information workers. Conducted over six weeks with 25 participants (15 treatment, 10 control), the study measured multiple dimensions of wellbeing and work engagement through repeated surveys.

## Key Findings

- Treatment participants using automatically scheduled focus time showed significantly higher wellbeing compared to controls
- Wellbeing improvements included increased excitement, relaxation, and satisfaction
- Treatment participants reported decreased anger, frustration, tiredness, and stress
- Work engagement also improved in the treatment group both over the study weeks and in pre-post comparisons
- The focus time service automatically identified and protected calendar blocks for uninterrupted work, reducing the cognitive burden of self-scheduling
- Notifications were silenced during focus blocks, creating a digital boundary against interruptions

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's smart scheduling and calendar blocking features, which use AI to analyze calendar events and generate blocking schedules for focus time. This study provides experimental evidence that the core mechanism behind MAON's calendar-blocking approach -- automatically protecting time for focused work -- leads to measurable improvements in wellbeing and reductions in stress. The finding that automated scheduling (rather than manual time blocking) drives engagement supports MAON's AI-driven approach to generating smart schedules from calendar data.

## Methodology Notes

Experimental design with 15 treatment and 10 control participants over six weeks. Treatment group used Microsoft Viva Focus Time service integrated with Outlook calendars. Wellbeing and work engagement measured through repeated survey assessments. Relatively small sample size limits statistical power; conducted with information workers at a single technology company, which may limit generalizability to other populations.
