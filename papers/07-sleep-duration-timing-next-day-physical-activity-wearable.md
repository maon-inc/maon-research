## Summary

A large-scale wearable sensor study investigating how sleep duration and timing predict next-day physical activity levels. The primary study used nearly 6 million nights of WHOOP data from approximately 20,000 participants over one year, with replication in approximately 6,000 Fitbit users from the All of Us Research Program.

## Key Findings

- Both average sleep patterns and night-to-night changes in sleep are significantly associated with next-day physical activity
- Participants with a typical bedtime around 9pm logged about 30 more minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) daily compared to those staying up until 1am
- Within-person changes show that earlier-than-usual bedtimes with maintained sleep duration produced the highest next-day activity levels
- Shorter-than-usual sleep and earlier-than-usual bedtimes were both associated with higher MVPA and total physical activity the following day
- Going to sleep earlier than usual may be an effective strategy to maintain normal sleep duration while optimizing next-day physical activity
- Results replicated across two independent datasets (WHOOP and Fitbit), strengthening generalizability

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's behavioral loops detection and cross-domain pattern analysis. The app already tracks sleep via Apple Health and activity data, and this paper provides evidence for the sleep-to-activity directional relationship that MAON's cognitive score and story cards could surface. The proactive agent could use sleep timing data to predict and encourage next-day activity.

## Methodology Notes

Two independent longitudinal studies: (1) ~20,000 WHOOP users over 1 year (~6 million nights), (2) ~6,000 Fitbit users from the All of Us Research Program. Both used consumer wearables for continuous data collection. Within-person analysis design controls for stable individual differences. Published in PNAS, a top-tier journal.
