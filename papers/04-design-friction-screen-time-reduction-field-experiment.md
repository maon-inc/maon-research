## Summary

A pre-registered randomized field experiment (N=112) compared two digital strategies for reducing smartphone screen time: a design friction intervention (activating grayscale mode) and a goal-setting intervention (self-commitment to time limits), against a control condition (self-monitoring only). The design friction approach produced the most immediate and significant reduction in objectively measured screen time.

## Key Findings

- The design friction (grayscale) intervention led to an immediate, significant reduction in objectively measured screen time compared to control
- The goal-setting intervention (time limit commitments) produced a smaller and more gradual reduction in screen time
- Self-monitoring alone (control condition) did not produce meaningful reductions
- Design friction works by reducing the visual appeal and reward salience of smartphone content, making habitual checking less reinforcing
- The study demonstrates that environmental modifications to the device itself are more effective than cognitive strategies like goal-setting
- Results were measured using objective screen time data rather than self-report, strengthening validity

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's smart schedule feature, which uses AI-generated blocking schedules to restrict social media access during designated time windows. This paper provides evidence that environmental friction interventions (like the app blocking MAON implements) are more effective than purely goal-based approaches. The finding that design friction works immediately supports MAON's automated blocking approach over voluntary limit-setting. MAON could also consider integrating grayscale mode triggers as an additional intervention layer.

## Methodology Notes

Pre-registered randomized field experiment with N=112 participants. Three conditions: design friction (grayscale mode), goal-setting (time limit commitments), and control (self-monitoring). Screen time was measured objectively through device tracking. Published in Cyberpsychology, Behavior, and Social Networking (2023).
