## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 23 randomized controlled trials (4,566 participants) evaluating wearable activity tracker-based interventions for physical activity, body composition, and physical function in community-dwelling older adults. The review found that tracker-based interventions improved physical activity levels, particularly step counts and activity time, but did not significantly affect body composition or physical function.

## Key Findings

- Wearable activity tracker interventions significantly increased daily step counts (moderate certainty evidence) and physical activity time (low certainty evidence) compared to usual care.
- The most notable improvements in physical activity were observed immediately after the intervention period.
- No significant effects on body composition or physical function were detected (low to moderate certainty evidence).
- Wearable tracker-based interventions were at least as effective as conventional behavioral change techniques, tailored exercises, and prescribed physical activity programs.
- 23 RCTs with 4,566 participants were included, providing substantial evidence for wearable-based activity promotion.
- Self-monitoring through wearable feedback appeared to be a key mechanism driving increased physical activity levels.

## MAON Applicability

Supports MAON's inclusion of daily step counts as a core component of the cognitive health score. The evidence that wearable self-monitoring increases physical activity validates MAON's approach of tracking and displaying step data. The finding that tracker interventions are comparable to traditional behavioral interventions supports using MAON as a standalone wellness tool. The step target of 10,000 used in MAON's health domain scoring aligns with common targets in these trials.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Searched PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, and CENTRAL through January 2025. Included 23 RCTs with 4,566 community-dwelling older adults. Risk of bias assessed using Cochrane RoB 2.0. GRADE framework used for certainty of evidence.
