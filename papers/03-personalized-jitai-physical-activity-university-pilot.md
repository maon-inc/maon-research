## Summary

A pilot study examining the effectiveness of personalized intervention criteria (PIC) versus uniform intervention criteria (UIC) in a mobile just-in-time adaptive intervention designed to increase physical activity among university students. Participants wore a wrist activity monitor for 2 weeks, with PIC derived from each individual's baseline movement and sedentary patterns.

## Key Findings

- Both PIC-based and UIC-based JITAIs significantly increased physical activity in the first hour after delivery (P<.001 for both)
- PIC outperformed UIC on all activity measures in the first hour: more calories burned (P=.02), more steps taken (P=.007), and greater distance moved (P=.003)
- 28 healthy university students (ages 18-23) were analyzed, with personalized thresholds calibrated from week-1 baseline data
- The mean distance moved and sedentary time per hour for each participant were used to derive personalized triggers
- Results support precision health approaches where intervention timing and thresholds are tailored to the individual
- Personalized criteria appear to produce more effective just-in-time prompts than population-level defaults

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's proactive agent and JITAI system. The finding that personalized thresholds outperform uniform ones supports MAON's approach of using individual baseline data (HRV, sleep, activity patterns) to determine when and how to reach out to users. The eval framework for proactive decisions could incorporate similar personalized trigger calibration.

## Methodology Notes

Pilot study with 28 healthy university students (12 female, ages 18-23). Two-week protocol with week 1 as baseline and week 2 as intervention. Wrist-worn activity monitors used for both baseline measurement and intervention triggering. Small sample size limits generalizability but the within-person design strengthens causal inference.
