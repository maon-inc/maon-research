## Summary

This scoping review systematically searched 7 databases and included 42 peer-reviewed studies that used passive sensing from wearables or smartphones combined with machine learning to monitor clinically diagnosed mental disorders, including depression and anxiety. While passive sensing and ML demonstrated promising accuracy (e.g., CNN-LSTM achieving 92.16% in anxiety detection), the evidence remains constrained by methodological heterogeneity, small samples, and ethical gaps.

## Key Findings

- Forty-two studies met inclusion criteria across 7 databases, representing the growing body of evidence for passive digital phenotyping in mental health.
- Convolutional neural network and long short-term memory (CNN-LSTM) architectures achieved up to 92.16% accuracy in anxiety detection from passive sensor data.
- The most commonly collected passive sensing modalities included accelerometry (physical activity), location/GPS, screen usage, sleep patterns, and communication metadata.
- Depression and anxiety were the most frequently studied conditions, aligning with their high prevalence and the availability of validated clinical instruments.
- Methodological limitations included small sample sizes, high risk of bias, lack of standardized preprocessing pipelines, and limited model interpretability.
- Ethical considerations around continuous passive monitoring (consent, data ownership, privacy) remain underaddressed in most studies.

## MAON Applicability

Validates MAON's overall approach of using passive wearable and smartphone sensing combined with AI/ML to monitor mental health. The review's finding that multimodal passive data streams (activity, sleep, screen use, location) can detect depression and anxiety supports MAON's multi-domain cognitive score architecture. The high accuracy achieved by deep learning models suggests that MAON could incorporate more sophisticated ML-based anomaly detection in future iterations. The ethical considerations identified are relevant to MAON's design of user consent flows and data handling practices.

## Methodology Notes

Scoping review following PRISMA-ScR guidelines. Searched 7 databases for studies published between January 2015 and February 2025. Included 42 peer-reviewed studies using passive sensing from consumer wearables or smartphones with machine learning for clinically diagnosed mental health conditions. Published in JMIR, August 2025.
