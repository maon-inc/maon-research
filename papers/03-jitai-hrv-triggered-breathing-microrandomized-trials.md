## Summary

Two preregistered microrandomized trials (N=60 and N=50) tested whether transient wearable-detected drops in HRV could trigger 1-minute just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs) in everyday life. Low nonmetabolic HRV (AddHRVr) was used as a real-time trigger delivered by smartwatch. Brief breathing or attention-focusing exercises restored autonomic function and reduced perceived stress and rumination. This is the first demonstration that wearable HRV drops can reliably trigger JITAIs to stabilize cardiac autonomic function.

## Key Findings

- Transient reductions in HRV, independent of physical movement (AddHRVr metric), reliably identified moments of psychophysiological vulnerability
- Following HRV-triggered 1-minute interventions, perceived stress and rumination significantly declined in both trials
- Slow-paced breathing (0.1 Hz) and mindful attention both boosted autonomic flexibility when HRV was compromised
- Benefits emerged across both HRV-triggered and randomly timed prompts, though autonomic recovery was stronger for HRV-triggered delivery
- Individualized trigger thresholds (derived from a 1-day calibration period) improved detection specificity
- Effects replicated across two independent studies with distinct intervention types

## MAON Applicability

MAON's proactive agent cron queries HRV data from Apple Health and Garmin to decide when to send outreach messages. This paper provides direct methodological validation for that approach, showing that HRV-triggered timing is superior to random timing for intervention delivery. It also supports MAON's 4-7-8 and breathing session features as interventions to deploy following detected HRV dips.

## Methodology Notes

Two preregistered microrandomized trials. Study 1: N=60, slow-paced breathing (0.1 Hz). Study 2: N=50, slow breathing vs. external attention focus. 1-day calibration period followed by 3 days of triggered prompts. Published August 2025 in JMIR. High ecological validity (ambulatory, everyday life). RMSSD and frequency-domain HRV measures used.
