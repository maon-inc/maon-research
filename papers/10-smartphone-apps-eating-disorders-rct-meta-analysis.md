## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials evaluating smartphone applications for eating disorder symptoms, published in the International Journal of Eating Disorders in 2025. Seventeen studies met inclusion criteria, with fourteen included in quantitative synthesis, providing the most comprehensive meta-analytic evidence to date on mobile app-based interventions for eating disorders.

## Key Findings

- App-based interventions were more effective than controls in reducing global eating disorder symptomatology in non-formally diagnosed samples
- Significant reductions found for shape and weight concerns, dietary restraint, binge eating frequency, and compensatory behaviors
- Self-monitoring features within apps showed particular promise for tracking and reducing binge eating episodes
- Effect sizes varied by target population, with stronger effects in prevention/early intervention than in clinical treatment
- Digital interventions offer scalability advantages for eating disorder care, which has traditionally relied on specialist face-to-face treatment
- The review highlights the potential for smartphone apps to serve as adjuncts to traditional treatment or standalone early interventions

## MAON Applicability

While MAON does not directly target eating disorders, this meta-analysis validates the broader principle of smartphone-based self-monitoring for behavioral health conditions. The finding that self-monitoring features reduce binge eating parallels MAON's approach of tracking screen time usage to promote digital wellness. The evidence that app-based interventions work best for prevention and early intervention aligns with MAON's positioning as a wellness companion rather than a clinical treatment tool. If MAON expands its eating disorder support (domain 10), this review provides strong evidence for the app-based delivery model.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis of 17 RCTs (14 in quantitative synthesis). Published in the International Journal of Eating Disorders (2025, volume 58, issue 9, pages 1662-1682). Studies included various app-based interventions targeting eating disorder symptoms across clinical and non-clinical populations. Effect sizes calculated for multiple outcome domains including global symptomatology, body image, dietary restraint, and binge eating frequency.
