## Summary

A large-scale study providing normative reference values for daily-life gait biomarkers derived from wrist-worn motion sensors. Data from 92,022 UK Biobank participants aged 45 to 79 wearing wrist accelerometers for at least three days were analyzed using the validated Watch Walk algorithm. The study established population norms for 20 gait and sleep biomarkers, including walking speed, step regularity, step count, and step-time variability.

## Key Findings

- Population mean maximal walking speed was 1.49 m/s and usual walking speed was 1.15 m/s
- Average daily step count was 7,749 steps across the cohort
- Step regularity averaged 65%, with women walking more regularly but slower than men
- All gait metrics showed significant age-related decline across the 45 to 79 age range
- Step-time variability increased with age, reflecting declining gait quality
- The Watch Walk algorithm successfully extracted 20 distinct gait and sleep biomarkers from raw wrist accelerometer data

## MAON Applicability

MAON's baseline engine tracks walking speed, step length, walking asymmetry, walking double support time, walking steadiness, and stair ascent/descent speed. This study provides critical normative reference data that MAON could use to contextualize individual gait metrics against population-level baselines. The age- and sex-stratified norms enable personalized interpretation of gait quality changes, which MAON currently computes using 30-day rolling averages. Declines in gait quality are increasingly recognized as early digital biomarkers for cognitive and mood changes.

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional analysis of 92,022 UK Biobank participants wearing Axivity AX3 wrist accelerometers for 3+ days. Watch Walk algorithm validated against instrumented walkway. Age- and sex-stratified normative ranges reported. Limitation: population is predominantly middle-aged to older adults in the UK, which may limit generalizability to younger and more diverse populations.
