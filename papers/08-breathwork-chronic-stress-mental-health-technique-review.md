## Summary

This review examines the psychophysiological pathways and mechanisms underlying various breathwork techniques, evaluating whether the choice of specific breathing technique significantly impacts mental health outcomes. The authors critically assess the roles of breathing pace, ratio, breathing route, attention, and biofeedback use, arguing that most techniques share core neurophysiological mechanisms.

## Key Findings

- Most breathwork techniques share core neurophysiological mechanisms that benefit well-being, regardless of theoretical differences between specific techniques
- The primary mechanism of action centers on the influence of voluntary breath regulation on the autonomic nervous system, particularly vagal tone enhancement
- Key breathing variables that modulate outcomes include pace (breaths per minute), inhalation-to-exhalation ratio, nasal vs. oral route, and attentional focus
- Slow-paced breathing (~6 breaths per minute) consistently activates the parasympathetic nervous system across multiple technique variants
- Biofeedback (especially HRV-guided breathing) enhances the effectiveness of breathwork by providing real-time physiological feedback
- Extended exhalation patterns (common across 4-7-8 breathing, box breathing, and coherence breathing) share the mechanism of prolonging vagal activation during the exhale phase

## Methodology Notes

Narrative review covering literature from Scopus, Web of Science, and PubMed databases through January 2025. The review focuses on the mechanistic overlap between diverse breathing techniques rather than conducting a formal meta-analysis. Published by researchers from the Medical University of Bialystok, Poland.
