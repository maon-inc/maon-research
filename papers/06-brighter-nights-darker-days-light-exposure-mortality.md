## Summary

This large-scale prospective study analyzed approximately 13 million hours of wrist-worn light sensor data from nearly 89,000 individuals aged 40 and over from the UK Biobank. The study found that those with brighter nights and darker days had significantly higher risk of premature mortality, after accounting for sociodemographic and lifestyle factors, providing strong evidence that personal light exposure patterns measured by wearables predict health outcomes.

## Key Findings

- Approximately 13 million hours of wrist-worn light sensor data were captured from ~89,000 participants, making this one of the largest wearable-based light exposure studies to date
- Brighter nighttime light exposure and darker daytime exposure independently predicted increased all-cause mortality risk
- Effects persisted after controlling for sociodemographic factors, lifestyle variables, and pre-existing health conditions
- The findings support the circadian disruption hypothesis: misaligned light exposure disrupts circadian rhythms, contributing to adverse health outcomes
- Avoiding nighttime light and seeking daytime light were identified as accessible, cost-effective health recommendations
- Wrist-worn accelerometers with built-in light sensors proved feasible for capturing personal light exposure at population scale

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's circadian rhythm monitoring capabilities and the Clarity Score's health domain. While MAON currently focuses on HRV, sleep, and activity data, this study demonstrates that personal light exposure patterns are a powerful predictor of health outcomes and could represent a future data source. The finding that circadian-aligned light exposure improves health outcomes supports MAON's approach of monitoring and nudging users toward healthier daily routines, including sleep timing and activity patterns.

## Methodology Notes

Prospective cohort study using UK Biobank data from ~89,000 participants aged 40+. Light exposure measured via wrist-worn accelerometers with integrated light sensors. Approximately 13 million hours of light data analyzed. Multivariate Cox regression models adjusted for sociodemographic and lifestyle confounders. Published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS), 2024.
