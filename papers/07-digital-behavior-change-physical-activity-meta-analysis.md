## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis of 18 randomized controlled trials evaluating standalone digital behavior change interventions (apps, wearables, web platforms) for increasing physical activity. The pooled analysis found a significant but small positive effect (SMD=0.324) on physical activity levels, supporting the efficacy of digital tools as accessible interventions for promoting movement.

## Key Findings

- Standalone digital interventions significantly improved physical activity compared to controls (SMD=0.324)
- Effect sizes were modest, suggesting digital tools work best as part of broader intervention packages
- App-based and wearable-integrated interventions showed the most consistent effects
- Interventions incorporating goal-setting and self-monitoring features performed better
- The evidence was rated as low-certainty, highlighting the need for more rigorous trials
- Digital interventions offer scalability advantages over traditional in-person programs

## MAON Applicability

Supports MAON's approach of using digital tools (app notifications, cognitive score feedback, smart scheduling) to encourage healthier behavior patterns. The finding that self-monitoring and goal-setting features enhance effectiveness validates MAON's step tracking and activity scoring within the health domain. The modest effect size suggests MAON's multi-signal approach (combining activity with sleep, HRV, screen time, calendar, and location) may be more effective than single-domain interventions.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Included 18 RCTs of standalone digital behavior change interventions (no human coaching component). Published in npj Digital Medicine in 2025. Pooled standardized mean differences calculated for physical activity outcomes. Evidence certainty rated using GRADE framework. Heterogeneity across studies noted as a limitation.
