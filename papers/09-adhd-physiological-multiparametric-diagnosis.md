## Summary

An observational study using non-invasive wearable sensors to analyze HRV, EDA, respiration, and skin temperature in 69 treatment-naive ADHD children vs. 29 controls. Demonstrates measurable autonomic differences.

## Key Findings

- ADHD children showed increased parasympathetic (higher HRV) and reduced sympathetic (lower EDA) activation
- Multiparametric approach provides richer characterization than single-signal methods
- Physiological parameters can support ADHD assessment alongside behavioral measures
- Treatment-naive children show baseline autonomic dysregulation before medication
- Wearable sensors are feasible for non-invasive ADHD physiological assessment

## MAON Applicability

Provides a physiological signature of ADHD detectable by wearable sensors. While MAON lacks EDA, it can use HRV patterns and activity data. The finding that ADHD shows distinct autonomic profiles helps MAON calibrate its signal interpretation for ADHD users.

## Methodology Notes

Published in Frontiers in Psychiatry (2024). 69 ADHD children, 29 controls. Treatment-naive population. Multiparametric wearable assessment. Open access.
