## Summary

Using data from two cycles of the National Health and Nutritional Examination Survey (NHANES 2011-2014), this study examined the association between objectively measured sleep regularity, sleep duration, and depression severity in a nationally representative US sample of 7,297 participants. The sleep regularity index (SRI) was calculated from physical activity monitors worn for seven days, providing objective rather than self-reported sleep data.

## Key Findings

- Lower sleep regularity index scores were significantly associated with higher depression severity in fully adjusted models
- Reduced subjective night sleep was also independently associated with greater depression severity
- A significant sex-by-SRI interaction revealed that the inverse relationship between sleep regularity and depression was stronger in females than males
- Females exhibited higher overall depression scores compared to males
- Daytime sleep (napping) was negatively correlated with sleep regularity
- The study supports sleep regularity as a modifiable risk factor for depression, independent of sleep duration
- Findings held after controlling for demographic, socioeconomic, and health-related confounders

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's sleep monitoring and cognitive scoring features. MAON tracks sleep through Apple Health and Garmin integration, giving it the ability to compute sleep regularity metrics alongside duration. This paper supports incorporating a sleep regularity index into MAON's cognitive score algorithm and daily summaries, as regularity may be as important as -- or more important than -- total sleep duration for mental health outcomes. The sex-specific findings could inform personalized threshold settings in MAON's scoring system.

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES data (2011-2014 cycles) with 7,297 participants wearing wrist accelerometers for seven days. Depression assessed via PHQ-9 questionnaire. Nationally representative sample provides strong external validity. The SRI was computed from objective accelerometer data rather than self-report, enhancing measurement reliability. The cross-sectional design limits causal inference, but the large, representative sample compensates with statistical power and generalizability.
