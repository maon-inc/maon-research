## Summary

Scoping review of wearable device capabilities for predicting chronic pain, covering sensor types, ML models, and key physiological variables. Found that HR, HRV, accelerometry, and electrodermal activity are the most effective wearable-derived features for pain prediction.

## Key Findings

- Accelerometers and optical HR sensors are the most widely used wearable sensors for pain monitoring
- Key predictive variables: movement intensity, heart rate, HRV, and electrodermal activity
- Random Forest algorithms showed particular effectiveness for pain classification
- Smartwatch data (resting HR, HRV, step counts) over 6 months discriminated pain phenotypes with 82% accuracy in 261 patients
- Model performance varied widely across studies (37.7% to 91.7% accuracy)
- Temporal trajectory features, especially short-term dynamics, outperformed global summary measures

## MAON Applicability

MAON users may self-report chronic pain. MAON collects HR, HRV, steps, and activity that overlap with pain-predictive signals. MAON could detect pain flare patterns through changes in activity levels, HRV drops, and disrupted sleep, offering holistic monitoring rather than pain-specific diagnosis.

Published in Frontiers in Digital Health (2025). Scoping review covering multiple databases. Covers sensor capabilities, ML approaches, and data security. Open access.
