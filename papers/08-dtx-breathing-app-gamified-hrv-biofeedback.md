## Summary

A study on the design and assessment of a digital therapeutics (DTx) mobile app for guided deep breathing, incorporating gamified and affordance-driven design with personalized machine learning biofeedback. The app uses Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficient (MFCC) analysis for accurate breathing detection and HRV-based outcome measurement. A randomized controlled trial with 70 participants demonstrated significant HRV stabilization and increased user engagement.

## Key Findings

- The intervention group showed pronounced stabilization of SDNN (Standard Deviation of NN Intervals), indicating significant stress alleviation compared to the control group
- Gamified breathing guidance with real-time biofeedback increased perceived enjoyment by 22% compared to traditional image-based guidance
- Positive attitudes toward the app increased by 10.69% in the intervention group
- MFCC-driven personalized ML enabled accurate breathing detection and adaptive pacing
- The DTx approach successfully bridged clinical breathing techniques with consumer-accessible mobile delivery
- HRV metrics (specifically SDNN) served as objective markers of breathing intervention effectiveness

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's breathing activity feature. The paper validates the approach of delivering guided breathing via mobile app and measuring effectiveness through HRV. MAON already tracks HRV via Apple Health and offers breathing sessions. This paper provides evidence for gamification approaches and personalized biofeedback that could enhance MAON's breathing feature. The SDNN stabilization finding supports using HRV as the outcome measure for breathing interventions within the app.

## Methodology Notes

Randomized controlled trial with 70 participants split into intervention and control groups. The intervention group used the gamified DTx app while the control group used an existing image-based breathing app. Outcomes measured via HRV (SDNN) using wearable sensors and self-reported engagement scales. Published in Frontiers in Digital Health. Limitation: relatively small sample, short-term assessment.
