## Summary

This study examined physiological responses during mealtimes in individuals with eating disorders compared to healthy controls. Using wearable sensors to measure heart rate and other autonomic markers, the research investigated whether mealtime-specific physiological patterns could distinguish eating disorder patients from controls, providing evidence for wearable-based detection of eating disorder behaviors in naturalistic settings.

## Key Findings

- Individuals with eating disorders showed a significant increase in heart rate before lunch compared to healthy controls, indicating anticipatory autonomic arousal.
- No significant effect on heart rate was observed during or after lunch, suggesting the physiological distress is primarily anticipatory rather than reactive.
- Mealtimes are highly distressing for individuals with eating disorders, and this distress manifests as measurable autonomic activation.
- The pattern of pre-meal physiological arousal could serve as a passive biomarker for detecting eating disorder-related distress.
- Wearable-detected heart rate changes around mealtimes may complement self-report measures for monitoring eating disorder symptoms.
- Continuous physiological monitoring captures mealtime-specific patterns that periodic clinical assessments miss.

## Methodology Notes

Comparative study measuring physiological responses (primarily heart rate) in eating disorder patients and healthy controls during structured mealtimes. Used wearable physiological sensors. Published 2025 in European Eating Disorders Review.
