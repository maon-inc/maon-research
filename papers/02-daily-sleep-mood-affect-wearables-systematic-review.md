## Summary

A systematic review of 121 studies examining the bidirectional relationship between sleep and mood/affect using digital technologies and wearables. Found that sleep is a stronger predictor of next-day affect than the reverse, and that consumer sleep tracking shows positive associations between total sleep time and next-day mood.

## Key Findings

- Sleep disturbances (poor quality, short duration) consistently predict worse next-day positive and negative affect
- Sleep is a stronger predictor of subsequent daytime affect than daytime affect is of subsequent sleep
- 52% of consumer sleep tracking studies showed a positive link between total sleep time and next-day mood
- Bidirectional sleep-affect associations found across both affective disorders and healthy participants
- Digital technologies enable ecological momentary assessment of sleep-mood dynamics in real-world settings

## MAON Applicability

Directly supports MAON's ability to flag poor sleep nights and anticipate next-day mood impacts. MAON could proactively message users after detecting poor sleep to offer support before mood deterioration occurs. Validates the sleep-first approach in MAON's pattern hierarchy.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review of 121 studies published in Sensors (2024). Screened 3,024 records. Covers both research-grade actigraphy and consumer wearables. Includes healthy and clinical populations.
