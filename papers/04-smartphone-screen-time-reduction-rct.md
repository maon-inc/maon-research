## Summary

A non-blinded parallel RCT at the University of Applied Sciences Krems in Austria randomized 111 healthy students to either reduce smartphone screen time to 2 hours per day for 3 weeks or continue normal use. The intervention produced small-to-medium effect improvements in depressive symptoms, sleep quality, stress, and wellbeing. However, after the intervention ended, screen time rapidly rebounded and mental health indicators began reverting, highlighting the need for ongoing behavioral support.

## Key Findings

- Three-week screen time reduction to 2 hours per day produced significant improvements across depressive symptoms (PHQ-9), sleep quality (ISI), wellbeing (WHO-5), and perceived stress (PSQ)
- Effect sizes were small to medium, suggesting practical as well as statistical significance
- Benefits began emerging within the first week and peaked at the 3-week post-intervention assessment
- After the intervention ended, screen time increased rapidly and mental health gains partially reversed by 6-week follow-up
- The study provides causal evidence rather than merely correlational support for the screen time and mental health link
- Mean baseline screen time in the sample was 276 minutes per day (over 4.5 hours)

## MAON Applicability

This trial directly supports MAON's screen time monitoring and app-blocking features. The causal relationship between screen time reduction and improved depression, sleep, and stress is the mechanistic backbone of MAON's screen time intervention tools. The rebound effect underscores why MAON's continuous monitoring and proactive agent outreach are necessary, not just a one-time reduction intervention.

## Methodology Notes

Non-blinded, parallel RCT. N=111 (58 intervention, 53 control). Participants were healthy university students (mean age 22.68 years, 70 female). Intervention: smartphone use limited to 2 hours per day for 3 weeks. Validated scales used at baseline, post-intervention (t1), and 6-week follow-up (t2). Published February 2025 in BMC Medicine.
