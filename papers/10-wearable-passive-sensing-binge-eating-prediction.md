## Summary

A protocol paper describing an observational study using wrist-worn passive sensors (HRV, electrodermal activity, skin temperature) combined with ecological momentary assessment (EMA) to predict binge eating episodes triggered by negative affect. The study addresses the limitation that standard EMA (5-6 surveys per day) cannot capture real-time physiological arousal preceding binge episodes. Recruitment ran from January 2023 to March 2024.

## Key Findings

- Negative affect is a well-established proximal trigger for binge eating, and the affect regulation model positions elevated negative affect as increasing momentary binge risk
- Standard EMA surveys are insufficient for capturing continuous physiological arousal states that precede binge episodes
- Wrist-worn sensors measuring HRV, electrodermal activity, and skin temperature can passively capture affect-related physiological arousal without burdening participants
- The study design combines passive sensing with meal-triggered EMAs and EMA-assessed affect ratings to build real-time predictive models
- If validated, passive physiological sensing could enable JITAI delivery precisely when binge risk is highest
- The transdiagnostic design includes both bulimia nervosa and binge-eating disorder populations

## MAON Applicability

This study validates the physiological signals MAON monitors (HRV, skin temperature from Apple Watch and Garmin) as candidates for binge-eating risk detection. MAON's proactive agent cron, which queries health and behavioral data to decide when to intervene, could incorporate HRV and skin temperature patterns as binge risk signals for users with disordered eating. The JITAI framing also aligns with MAON's overall intervention delivery architecture.
