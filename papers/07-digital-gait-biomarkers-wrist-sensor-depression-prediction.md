## Summary

A large-scale study using UK Biobank data (n=72,359) examined whether digital gait biomarkers derived from wrist-worn accelerometers could predict incident depression over a follow-up period of up to 9 years. Gait quality and quantity metrics were significant predictors of future depressive episodes, demonstrating the potential of passively collected movement data as digital biomarkers for mental health.

## Key Findings

- Among 72,359 participants, 1,332 (1.8%) developed incident depressive episodes over a mean follow-up of 7.4 years
- Digital gait quality and quantity biomarkers were significant predictors of incident depression
- Both gait speed and gait regularity metrics contributed to predictive models
- Walk-related arm movement proportions and walk length distributions provided additional predictive value
- Wrist-worn accelerometer data collected over just 7 days at baseline was sufficient for meaningful prediction
- Results held in multivariable models adjusting for demographic and clinical covariates

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's health domain scoring which tracks step counts and physical activity via Apple Health. The finding that movement quality (not just quantity) predicts depression supports richer activity analysis beyond simple step counting. The use of wrist-worn accelerometer data aligns with Apple Watch data that MAON already collects. Could inform future features that analyze walking patterns and movement quality as part of the cognitive score.

## Methodology Notes

Prospective cohort study using UK Biobank data with 72,359 participants who wore wrist accelerometers (Axivity AX3) for up to 7 days. Digital gait biomarkers were extracted using the Watch Walk algorithm. Cox proportional-hazard regression models analyzed associations between baseline gait parameters and diagnosed incident depressive episodes for up to 9 years of follow-up. Large sample size and long follow-up strengthen the findings.
