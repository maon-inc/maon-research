## Summary

This randomized clinical trial evaluated the efficacy of Resilience: eDBT, the first dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) skills training smartphone application specifically designed for binge-spectrum eating disorders. Participants with recurrent binge eating were randomized to either the app intervention or a waitlist control group over a 6-week period.

## Key Findings

- The intervention group showed significantly greater reductions in objective binge eating episodes compared to waitlist (incidence rate ratio = 0.69)
- Eating disorder psychopathology decreased with a medium effect size (d = -0.68) in the intervention group
- The app focused on teaching emotion regulation skills adapted from DBT while integrating core CBT strategies
- Dropout rate was high (48%) in the intervention group, a common challenge in digital eating disorder interventions
- Engagement with the app decreased over the study period, suggesting the need for engagement-sustaining features
- Results support the potential of standalone app-based DBT for reducing binge eating, though engagement remains a key challenge

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's intervention and chat-based support features. The DBT emotion regulation approach could inform MAON's proactive agent interventions, particularly when the app detects physiological patterns (via HRV, activity changes) that may precede disordered eating behaviors. The engagement dropout findings are important for MAON's own user retention strategy, and the app's focus on just-in-time skill delivery aligns with MAON's JITAI approach to wellness interventions.

## Methodology Notes

Randomized clinical trial with participants experiencing recurrent binge eating (at least weekly for the past 3 months). Participants were randomized to use the Resilience: eDBT app or a waitlist control for 6 weeks. Primary outcomes included objective binge eating episode frequency and eating disorder psychopathology scores. The study was conducted by researchers at Deakin University, Australia.
