## Summary

A validation study examining whether composite stress scores from consumer wearables (primarily HRV-based) align with self-reported stress experiences in a real-world setting. The study tested Dutch police officers wearing consumer devices while working, finding that wearable-derived stress metrics did not consistently correspond with users' subjective experiences of stress and recovery, raising questions about the validity of proprietary composite scoring algorithms.

## Key Findings

- Wearable-based stress scores did not consistently align with users' self-reported experiences of stress and recovery in a real-world occupational setting
- Composite health scores from consumer wearables rely primarily on HRV and heart rate but use unpublished, proprietary algorithms, making independent validation difficult
- The disconnect between physiological measurements and subjective stress experience highlights the challenge of translating raw biometric data into meaningful user-facing metrics
- Physical activity and posture changes can confound HRV-based stress scores, as these factors also alter autonomic nervous system activity
- The study suggests that contextual information (time of day, activity type, sleep status) is critical for interpreting wearable stress metrics accurately
- Real-world validation studies with occupational populations provide more ecologically valid evidence than controlled laboratory studies

## MAON Applicability

Important cautionary evidence for MAON's cognitive score algorithm and any stress-related features. The finding that raw composite scores can misalign with lived experience underscores the value of MAON's multi-signal approach (integrating HRV with sleep, activity, calendar data, and user context) rather than relying on a single stress metric. The study supports MAON's design choice to combine physiological data with contextual signals (screen time, calendar events, location) for more meaningful wellness insights. MAON should consider transparent communication about what its scores represent and their limitations.

## Methodology Notes

Field validation study with Dutch police officers using consumer wearable devices during regular work shifts. Compared wearable-derived composite stress scores against self-reported stress measures collected at multiple time points. Real-world occupational setting provides high ecological validity. Published in Psychophysiology (2025).
