## Summary

An intensive longitudinal study examining person-specific (idiographic) relationships between smartphone use patterns and mental health. Demonstrates that the smartphone-mental health relationship is highly individual, requiring personalized analysis rather than population-level generalizations.

## Key Findings

- Smartphone-mental health relationships are highly person-specific
- Population-level associations mask substantial individual heterogeneity
- Some individuals show negative effects of smartphone use while others show neutral or positive effects
- Person-specific models outperform group-level models for predicting mental health outcomes
- Personalized thresholds are needed rather than universal screen time limits

## MAON Applicability

Strongly supports MAON's personalized baseline approach. Rather than applying universal screen time thresholds, MAON should detect changes relative to each user's own patterns. A user whose social media time doubles from their baseline is more informative than absolute hours.
