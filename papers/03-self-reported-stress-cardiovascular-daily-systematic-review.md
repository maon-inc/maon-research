## Summary

A systematic review examining the association between self-reported stress and cardiovascular measures (HR, HRV, blood pressure) in daily life settings. Finds that heart rate is the most consistently associated physiological measure with perceived stress.

## Key Findings

- Among physiological measures, heart rate showed the most consistent statistically significant association with self-reported stress
- HRV associations with perceived stress are less consistent in daily life than in laboratory settings
- Contextual factors (e.g., perceived control) moderate the physiological-subjective stress relationship
- Heart rate is a simpler and more reliable marker of stress in ecological settings than HRV
- The relationship between physiology and perceived stress is highly individual

## MAON Applicability

Suggests MAON should weight HR alongside HRV for stress detection rather than relying on HRV alone. The finding that HR is more consistently associated with self-reported stress in daily life supports MAON's use of HR mean/std/range features in its stress model.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review published in PLOS ONE (2021). Covers studies using ambulatory/daily life measurements. Includes multiple cardiovascular measures. Open access.
