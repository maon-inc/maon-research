## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis evaluating the effectiveness of generative AI (GenAI) mental health chatbots across 26 studies, with a focused meta-analysis of 14 randomized controlled trials (N=6,314). The study synthesizes technical features, treatment designs, and sample characteristics of GenAI chatbot interventions for reducing negative mental health outcomes including depression and anxiety.

## Key Findings

- Meta-analysis of 14 RCTs showed a statistically significant effect (ES=0.30, p=.047, N=6,314), indicating GenAI chatbots are on average effective in reducing negative mental health issues
- The literature search returned 5,555 records; 26 studies met inclusion criteria with 15 RCTs, 5 quasi-experimental, 3 mixed-methods, 2 survey, and 1 observational design
- LLM-based chatbots surged to 45% of new studies in 2024, up from near-zero before 2023, marking a rapid shift from rule-based systems
- Only 16% of LLM-based chatbot studies underwent clinical efficacy testing, with 77% still in early validation phases
- GenAI chatbots demonstrated capability for personalized, context-aware therapeutic interactions that adapt to user needs in real time
- Significant heterogeneity across studies in chatbot design, therapeutic modality, and outcome measures limits definitive conclusions about optimal implementation

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's AI-powered conversational agent delivered via iMessage through the Sendblue integration. The effect size of 0.30 provides evidence supporting the therapeutic value of AI chatbot interactions for mental health. The finding that LLM-based approaches are rapidly overtaking rule-based systems validates MAON's use of AI Gateway and structured output generation for its agent loop. The gap in clinical validation of LLM chatbots highlights an opportunity for MAON to contribute real-world evidence.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines with comprehensive database search completed March 2025. Meta-analysis restricted to RCTs with validated mental health outcome measures and appropriate control groups. The 14 included RCTs had a combined sample of 6,314 participants. Study quality varied; most LLM-based studies were early-stage with limited follow-up periods.
