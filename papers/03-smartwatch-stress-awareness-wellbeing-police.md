## Summary

This longitudinal mixed-design study examined whether a 5-week wearable-based intervention using smartwatches and a smartphone app could improve stress awareness, self-efficacy, and well-being in 95 Dutch police officers. The basic wearable intervention produced small but significant improvements, though adding ecological momentary assessment and peer support did not enhance effectiveness further.

## Key Findings

- A 5-week smartwatch-based stress monitoring intervention produced small to medium improvements in stress awareness, self-efficacy, and well-being (Hedges' g = 0.25-0.46, p < 0.05)
- The basic "off-the-shelf" wearable intervention was effective on its own
- Adding EMA questionnaires, weekly personalized feedback reports, and peer support groups did not improve the intervention's effectiveness beyond the base wearable
- Wearable-derived composite stress scores did not always align with participants' self-reported stress experiences
- Self-monitoring through wearable data fostered increased stress awareness even when score alignment was imperfect
- 79 of 95 enrolled participants completed the study, indicating good retention

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's core value proposition of using wearable data for stress awareness and wellbeing. The finding that simple wearable-based self-monitoring improves stress outcomes without requiring complex add-ons validates MAON's approach. The disconnect between wearable stress scores and subjective experience highlights the importance of MAON's AI companion in contextualizing health data for users rather than presenting raw scores alone.

## Methodology Notes

Longitudinal mixed-design study with 95 Dutch police officers (79 completed). Five-week intervention with two conditions: basic wearable monitoring versus enhanced monitoring with EMA, personalized reports, and peer groups. Well-powered for the primary comparison. Occupational sample limits generalizability but provides a high-stress population relevant to burnout and stress management research.
