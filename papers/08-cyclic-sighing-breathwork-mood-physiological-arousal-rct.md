## Summary

A remote, randomized controlled study (n=108) compared three daily 5-minute breathwork exercises (cyclic sighing, box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation with retention) to mindfulness meditation over one month. Cyclic sighing, which emphasizes prolonged exhalations, produced the greatest improvements in positive affect and the largest reductions in respiratory rate and physiological arousal compared to all other conditions, including mindfulness meditation.

## Key Findings

- Cyclic sighing (prolonged exhalation emphasis) was significantly more effective at improving mood than mindfulness meditation
- All breathwork conditions improved mood and reduced anxiety over time, but controlled breathing outperformed mindfulness meditation overall
- Breathwork produced greater reductions in respiratory rate than mindfulness meditation
- Benefits increased with number of days practiced, suggesting a dose-response relationship
- Even 5 minutes of daily practice was sufficient to produce measurable improvements
- Physiological arousal (heart rate, HRV, respiratory rate) decreased more in the breathwork groups

## Methodology Notes

Remote RCT with 108 participants randomized to four groups: cyclic sighing (n=27), box breathing (n=19), cyclic hyperventilation (n=32), and mindfulness meditation (n=22). Participants completed daily 5-minute sessions over 28 days with pre/post questionnaires including State Anxiety Inventory and PANAS. Physiological data collected via wearable devices. Published by Stanford researchers including Andrew Huberman and David Spiegel.
