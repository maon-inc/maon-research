## Summary

An observational study examining the use of passively collected smartphone data to predict and monitor depressive symptoms in clinically diagnosed patients. The study analyzes GPS-derived location features including location entropy, circadian movement regularity, and routine indices to identify digital phenotyping markers that track with depression severity over time.

## Key Findings

- Location entropy (variability of time spent across location clusters) correlates with depressive symptom severity, with lower entropy indicating more restricted movement patterns
- Circadian movement regularity (consistency of 24-hour movement patterns) relates to depression: people with more regular patterns relative to their own average report lower depression scores
- Location variance and proportion of time spent at home are significant predictors of depressive symptom changes
- GPS-derived routine index captures day-to-day behavioral consistency that deteriorates during depressive episodes
- Temporal analysis reveals that circadian movement at the time of symptom reporting is associated with concurrent depression severity, though prospective prediction is more limited
- Combined smartphone modalities (location, activity, communication logs) outperform any single data source for depression monitoring

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's location tracking feature, which monitors visit patterns as a "routine" signal feeding into the cognitive score. The location entropy and routine index findings validate MAON's approach of using location regularity as a mental health indicator. Supports the design of MAON's location domain scoring, where permission status, event frequency, and upload recency contribute to a readiness/actual score reflecting behavioral regularity.

## Methodology Notes

Observational study with clinically diagnosed depression patients. Used passively collected smartphone GPS data analyzed over multiple weeks. Published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research (JMIR) in 2024.
