## Summary

A prospective study applying Fourier transform frequency-domain analysis to GPS-derived mobility patterns for distinguishing bipolar disorder from major depressive disorder and healthy controls. The study analyzed 5,177 person-days of data from 62 participants over observation periods ranging from 5 days to 6 months.

## Key Findings

- Maximum power spectra of location variance and entropy differed significantly between bipolar disorder and major depressive disorder groups
- The intensity (amplitude) of mobility patterns, rather than their frequency (periodicity), better differentiated bipolar disorder from major depressive disorder
- Frequency-domain analysis via Fourier transform captured cyclical mobility patterns that standard time-domain metrics may miss
- GPS-derived mobility features showed promise as objective biomarkers for mood disorder subtyping
- Integrating GPS data with ecological momentary assessments could enhance clinical assessment precision and provide early warnings for mood episodes
- The approach supports personalized intervention timing based on detected mobility pattern changes

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's location tracking and trend analysis features. The Fourier transform approach to detecting cyclical patterns in mobility data could enhance MAON's trend detection algorithms, particularly for identifying periodic mood fluctuations. The finding that mobility intensity is more diagnostically informative than periodicity could inform how MAON's data context builders weight location-derived features in the cognitive score.

## Methodology Notes

Prospective observational study with 62 participants (20 bipolar disorder, 27 major depressive disorder, 15 healthy controls). Collected continuous GPS data over 5 days to 6 months (5,177 person-days total). Applied Fourier transform to extract frequency-domain features from location variance and entropy time series. Relatively small sample limits generalizability.
