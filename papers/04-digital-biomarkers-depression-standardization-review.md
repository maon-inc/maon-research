## Summary

This systematic review analyzed 27 studies (from 161 identified records, 2019-2024) examining digital biomarkers for depression detection via passive smartphone and wearable data. It found that while ML classification models achieved accuracies up to 0.98, the field suffers from inconsistent outcome reporting and lack of standardization in feature engineering, hindering cross-study comparison.

## Key Findings

- Machine learning models for depression classification from passive data achieved accuracies up to 0.98, surpassing many standardized assessment methods
- Depressed participants showed reduced communication frequency, altered mobility patterns, and disrupted sleep tracked by passive sensing
- Routine irregularity was among the strongest digital biomarker associations with depression (r = 0.38, p < 0.001)
- Sleep disruption showed the second strongest association (r = 0.32, p < 0.001)
- Inconsistency of outcome reporting across studies prevents statistical conclusions about individual feature effectiveness
- The field urgently needs standardized feature engineering pipelines and reporting frameworks

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's multimodal approach combining health data, screen time, calendar, and location signals. The finding that routine irregularity is the strongest digital biomarker for depression supports MAON's location-based routine tracking feature. The call for standardization of feature engineering informs how MAON should approach its cognitive score computation and health data analysis pipeline.

## Methodology Notes

PRISMA-guided systematic review searching PubMed and PsycInfo for studies published 2019-2024. Started with 161 records, assessed 74 for quality, and included 27 in the final review. Focused on passive sensing (smartphone and wearable) with machine learning for depression classification. Rigorous quality assessment framework applied to all included studies.
