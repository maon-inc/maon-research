## Summary

This duoethnographic study explored how wearable-integrated LLM chatbots can provide personalized stress management interventions triggered by physiological data. Over 22 days, two researchers interacted with custom chatbots that responded to wearable-detected physiological prompts. Participants recorded stressor phrases and used them to seek tailored interventions from their LLM-powered chatbots, analyzing their experiences through autoethnographic diaries and weekly discussions focused on relevance, clarity, and impact of chatbot-generated interventions.

## Key Findings

- Wearable devices can trigger LLM-based chatbot interactions when physiological stress indicators are detected, enabling proactive rather than reactive support
- Custom LLM chatbots generated personalized stress interventions based on the specific stressor context reported by users
- The 22-day study period provided longitudinal insights into the evolving relationship between users and their AI stress management companions
- Autoethnographic diaries revealed how the relevance, clarity, and perceived impact of chatbot interventions changed over time
- The integration of physiological triggers with conversational AI represents a novel approach to just-in-time adaptive interventions
- Weekly reflective discussions between researchers highlighted both the potential and limitations of wearable-triggered AI support

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's core architecture of combining wearable physiological data with AI-powered proactive interventions. The study's model of using wearable-detected stress signals to trigger LLM chatbot conversations directly mirrors MAON's proactive agent cron system, which uses HRV and other health data to determine when to reach out to users. The focus on personalization based on individual stressor contexts aligns with MAON's approach of contextualizing AI responses with the user's specific health data from Apple Health and Garmin. The longitudinal nature of the study also provides insights relevant to MAON's ongoing user engagement strategy.

## Methodology Notes

Duoethnographic study with 2 researcher-participants over 22 days. Qualitative methodology using autoethnographic diaries and weekly reflective discussions. Published at CHI EA 2025 (Extended Abstracts), which undergoes peer review but at a lighter level than full CHI papers. Small sample size limits generalizability, but the depth of qualitative insight is valuable for design implications.
