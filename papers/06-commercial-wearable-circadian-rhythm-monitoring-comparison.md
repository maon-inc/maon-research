## Summary

This study evaluated the ability of commercial wearable devices to monitor circadian rhythms in real-world home settings using three data modalities: physical activity, heart rate, and core body temperature. Circadian rhythm acrophases derived from each modality were compared against gold-standard actigraphy and validated chronotype questionnaires.

## Key Findings

- Activity-based circadian acrophase from commercial smartwatches showed the strongest correlation with reference Actigraph measurements (r=0.96, p<0.001)
- Heart rate-based acrophase also correlated well (r=0.87, p<0.001), followed by core body temperature (r=0.79, p<0.001)
- All three modalities significantly correlated with self-reported chronotype questionnaire scores (r=-0.66 to -0.73, p<0.001)
- Commercial wearables can reliably estimate circadian timing without requiring specialized research-grade equipment
- Temperature-based monitoring provides a complementary signal that captures different aspects of circadian function compared to activity alone

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's circadian rhythm monitoring capabilities. The app collects activity data via Apple Health steps, heart rate data via resting HR and HRV, and could potentially incorporate temperature signals from Apple Watch. The finding that multiple wearable-derived signals can reliably estimate circadian timing supports MAON's multi-signal approach in the cognitive score feature, where health data from different modalities are composited into an overall wellness assessment.

## Methodology Notes

Longitudinal study with 36 participants wearing an Actigraph, a commercial smartwatch, and a core body temperature sensor simultaneously for two weeks in uncontrolled home settings. Circadian acrophases were computed using cosinor analysis and compared across devices and modalities.
