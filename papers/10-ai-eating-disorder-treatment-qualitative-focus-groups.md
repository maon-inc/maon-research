## Summary

This explorative qualitative study analyzed two interdisciplinary focus groups consisting of 22 eating disorder and AI professionals to explore how AI might support eating disorder treatment. Themes were categorized into opportunities, challenges, concerns, solutions, and evidence needed, providing a comprehensive overview of the clinical landscape for AI adoption in eating disorder care.

## Key Findings

- AI opportunities centered on enhancing clinical efficiency, supporting treatment delivery and continuous monitoring, and reducing human error in eating disorder care
- Challenges included barriers to adoption in clinical practice, questions around responsibility, and the need for explainability of AI decision-making
- Ethical and legal risks related to data sharing were a major concern among clinicians
- Proposed solutions emphasized human oversight, cross-sector collaboration between AI developers and clinicians, and clinician training on AI tools
- Participants highlighted the need for evidence on safety, accuracy, and rigorous scientific testing and validation before clinical deployment
- Bridging the communication gap between technical developers and clinical experts was identified as critical for developing usable and safe AI solutions

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's AI-driven approach to wellness monitoring. The findings about balancing AI automation with human oversight, ensuring explainability, and maintaining ethical data practices apply to MAON's use of AI for generating personalized health insights from wearable data. The emphasis on clinician-developer collaboration and validation requirements informs how MAON should position its research-backed AI features.

## Methodology Notes

Explorative qualitative analysis using hybrid thematic analysis (deductive coding combined with inductive theme development) in ATLAS.ti. Two focus groups with 22 total participants comprising both eating disorder clinicians and AI professionals from the Netherlands. Funded by ITEA (21016). Published in International Journal of Eating Disorders, February 2026.
