## Summary

Investigates the effects of caffeine intake on both subjective and objective (wearable-measured) sleep quality in young healthy adults. Found a discrepancy between subjective sleep worsening and objective wearable measures that showed no significant change.

## Key Findings

- Self-assessed sleep quality decreased after caffeine: poor sleep reports increased 8% after coffee, 20% after energy drinks
- Objective wearable measures (light/deep sleep duration, time awake, average HR) showed no significant changes
- Discrepancy between subjective and objective sleep measures highlights perception bias
- Caffeine at high doses (500mg) decreases HRV through sympathetic activation
- Individual sensitivity varies by age, gender, and health status
- Women may be more sensitive to caffeine's HRV effects than men

## MAON Applicability

MAON tracks both caffeine intake (via HealthKit nutrition data) and sleep quality (via Apple Watch). This paper supports MAON correlating caffeine consumption with subjective vs objective sleep discrepancies, and tracking HRV impacts of caffeine timing and dose.

## Methodology Notes

Published in Nutrients (2025). Used wearable sleep tracking alongside self-report surveys. Young healthy adult cohort. Compared coffee vs energy drink vs baseline periods. Open access.
