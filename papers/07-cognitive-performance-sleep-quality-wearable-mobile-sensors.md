## Summary

This 2-month field study investigates the relationship between objectively measured sleep quality (via a wearable sleep-tracking ring) and cognitive performance assessed through the Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) and smartphone typing metrics. The study demonstrates that specific sleep metrics significantly predict next-day cognitive performance and introduces smartphone typing as a continuous passive proxy for cognitive state.

## Key Findings

- Night-time heart rate, sleep latency, sleep timing, sleep restfulness, and sleep quantity all significantly influenced next-day cognitive performance
- Smartphone typing metrics (speed, accuracy, patterns) served as a viable passive proxy for cognitive performance measurement
- PVT reaction time correlated with wearable-derived sleep quality metrics, validating the ring-based approach
- The combination of wearable sleep data and mobile phone usage patterns provided a more complete picture of cognitive readiness than either source alone
- Sleep restfulness and timing were among the strongest predictors, supporting the importance of sleep architecture beyond mere duration
- The passive, continuous nature of both data sources makes this approach scalable for real-world monitoring

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's cognitive score feature and its integration of Apple Health and Garmin sleep data. The validated link between wearable sleep metrics and cognitive performance supports MAON's approach of generating cognitive readiness scores from sleep data. The smartphone typing proxy concept could inform future passive cognitive monitoring features. The specific sleep metrics identified as most predictive (heart rate, latency, timing, restfulness) align with data MAON already collects from Apple Health.

## Methodology Notes

Two-month field study with participants wearing a sleep-tracking ring (Oura-type) and completing daily PVT assessments. Smartphone keyboard data collected passively. Sample size not specified in available summaries but described as a field deployment. Mixed-methods analysis combining physiological sleep data with behavioral smartphone metrics. Published in a peer-reviewed ubiquitous computing journal (Springer).
