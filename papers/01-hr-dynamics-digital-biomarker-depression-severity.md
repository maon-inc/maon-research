## Summary

This 2-year longitudinal study in individuals with a history of recurrent major depressive disorder explored how intra-individual variations in daytime and nighttime heart rate parameters relate to depression severity. The study used consumer-grade wearable devices for continuous heart rate monitoring, demonstrating that both daytime and nighttime heart rate dynamics serve as viable digital biomarkers for tracking depression severity over time.

## Key Findings

- Lower resting heart rate variation (measured by standard deviation of HR during the day) is associated with higher depression severity
- Nocturnal mean heart rate increases with depression severity, providing a nighttime biomarker
- Both daytime and nighttime HR dynamics show clinically meaningful associations with PHQ-8 depression scores
- Intra-individual changes in HR dynamics track within-person fluctuations in depression severity over time
- The combination of daytime and nighttime HR features provides better depression tracking than either alone
- Consumer-grade wearable data is sufficient for detecting these depression-related HR patterns

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's use of resting heart rate in its cognitive scoring algorithm and daily health summaries. MAON collects continuous heart rate data through Apple Health and Garmin integrations, including nocturnal resting heart rate. This paper validates MAON's approach of using HR dynamics as a component of mental wellness assessment. The finding that both daytime variability and nighttime mean HR track depression severity supports MAON's multi-metric approach. The longitudinal design demonstrates that HR-based biomarkers can track within-person changes -- exactly the use case MAON targets with its daily cognitive scores and trend monitoring.

## Methodology Notes

Two-year prospective longitudinal study in participants with recurrent major depressive disorder. Continuous heart rate monitoring via consumer-grade wearable devices. Depression severity assessed regularly using PHQ-8. The longitudinal within-person design provides stronger evidence than cross-sectional studies for the utility of HR dynamics as ongoing depression biomarkers. Published in Psychological Medicine (impact factor ~7.7), a top-tier psychiatry journal. The 2-year follow-up period is notably longer than most digital biomarker studies.
