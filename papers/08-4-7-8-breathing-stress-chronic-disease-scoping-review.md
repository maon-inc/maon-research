## Summary

Scoping review of 15 studies published between 2013 and 2024 examining the 4-7-8 breathing technique for stress relief and quality-of-life improvement. The review synthesizes evidence across clinical populations including chronic and degenerative disease contexts, evaluating both psychological outcomes (stress, anxiety) and physiological mechanisms (HRV, blood pressure, parasympathetic activation).

## Key Findings

- The 4-7-8 technique consistently reduced self-reported stress and anxiety across multiple study populations and clinical contexts
- Cardiovascular markers improved with regular practice, including increased heart rate variability and reduced blood pressure
- The technique is adaptable to both clinical settings and community-based interventions, making it suitable for digital health delivery
- Healthy individuals showed preventive benefits from regular 4-7-8 practice, not just those with existing conditions
- The mechanism operates through parasympathetic activation via vagal pathways: the extended exhale phase (8 seconds) stimulates the vagus nerve and shifts autonomic balance toward rest-and-digest
- The inhale-hold-exhale ratio (4:7:8) appears optimized for activating the baroreflex and inducing respiratory sinus arrhythmia

## MAON Applicability

Directly validates MAON's implementation of the 4-7-8 breathing technique as its core breathing intervention (inhale 4s, hold 7s, exhale 8s with Live Activity support). The evidence for both stress reduction and cardiovascular improvement supports positioning the breathing feature as a scientifically backed wellness tool. The finding that the technique works for healthy individuals as a preventive measure aligns with MAON's general wellness audience rather than clinical-only use.

## Methodology Notes

Scoping review following Arksey and O'Malley framework. Searched PubMed, Scopus, and Google Scholar for studies published 2013-2024. Included 15 studies with heterogeneous designs (RCTs, quasi-experimental, observational). Limitations include the conference proceedings format, relatively small number of included studies, and variable study quality across the corpus.
