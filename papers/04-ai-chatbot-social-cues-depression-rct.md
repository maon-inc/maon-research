## Summary

An open-label randomized controlled trial comparing high-social-cue (HSC) and low-social-cue (LSC) AI chatbot designs for depression intervention in college students. The HSC chatbot included text, voice, and animations, while the LSC chatbot was text-only. The 16-week trial enrolled 84 students with baseline PHQ-9 scores of 9 or higher.

## Key Findings

- Chatbots with high social cues significantly outperformed text-only versions in reducing depression and anxiety symptoms
- High-social-cue design enhanced user adherence, satisfaction, and therapeutic alliance compared to text-only delivery
- Both conditions produced meaningful symptom reductions, but the multi-modal (text + voice + animation) approach was consistently superior
- The study demonstrates that how a chatbot communicates matters as much as what it communicates for mental health outcomes
- Therapeutic alliance ratings -- typically associated with human therapists -- were meaningfully high in the HSC condition
- 84 college students with clinically elevated depression (PHQ-9 >= 9) were randomized to HSC or LSC groups

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's iMessage-based interaction model. While MAON currently operates through text messages (inherently low social cue), this paper suggests that enriching messages with additional modalities could improve outcomes. The finding that text-only chatbots still reduce depression symptoms validates MAON's SMS-based approach, while pointing toward potential enhancements (e.g., multimedia messages, voice notes).

## Methodology Notes

Open-label RCT with 84 college students over 16 weeks. PHQ-9 used for depression screening (cutoff >= 9) and outcome measurement. Both groups received AI-driven therapeutic content; only the delivery modality differed. Limitations include open-label design (no blinding) and university student sample limiting generalizability.
