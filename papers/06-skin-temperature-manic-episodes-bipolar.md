## Summary

Demonstrates that manic episodes in bipolar disorder produce measurably higher daytime skin temperatures compared to euthymic states. This state-dependent temperature change is detectable via wrist-worn wearable devices and may serve as a digital biomarker for episode onset.

## Key Findings

- Patients in manic episodes exhibited higher daytime skin temperatures across most hours from mid-morning through evening
- Temperature elevations were state-dependent, resolving when mania subsided
- Wearable wrist temperature sensors could continuously monitor these changes
- Skin temperature may serve as an objective, passive biomarker for manic episode detection
- Findings align with hypermetabolic state during mania

## MAON Applicability

MAON collects nightly wrist temperature via Apple Watch. This paper suggests that elevated daytime/evening temperature patterns may indicate emerging manic episodes in bipolar users. MAON could flag sustained temperature elevations as a potential mood episode warning signal.

## Methodology Notes

Published in Journal of Affective Disorders (2025). Wearable continuous skin temperature monitoring. Compared manic episodes to euthymic baselines within subjects. Recent publication with high clinical relevance.
