## Summary

A meta-analysis of cohort studies examining the longitudinal relationship between screen time and depression risk. Cohort design provides stronger causal evidence than cross-sectional studies.

## Key Findings

- Longitudinal evidence supports a prospective association between higher screen time and increased depression risk
- The association holds across different types of screen activities
- Effect sizes are modest but consistent across studies
- Dose-response pattern observed with increasing screen time
- Gender and age moderate the relationship

## MAON Applicability

Supports MAON's use of screen time as a meaningful behavioral signal. The longitudinal evidence strengthens the case for MAON flagging sustained increases in screen time as a potential warning indicator for depression risk.

## Methodology Notes

Meta-analysis of cohort studies published in Frontiers in Psychiatry (2022). Longitudinal designs provide stronger evidence than cross-sectional. Open access.
