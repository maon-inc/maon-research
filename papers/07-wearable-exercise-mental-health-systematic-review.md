## Summary

A systematic review of 90 studies examining how wearable physiological monitoring captures the relationship between physical exercise and mental health outcomes. Covers literature from 2005 to 2024 and evaluates the current state of wearable technology for tracking exercise-related mental health benefits, identifying promising biomarkers and highlighting challenges in standardization.

## Key Findings

- Physical exercise consistently demonstrated positive impacts on mental health, particularly in improving mood, reducing anxiety, and mitigating stress
- Wearable physiological monitoring shows promise for understanding the exercise-mental health relationship at a granular, individual level
- Key biomarkers captured by wearables include heart rate variability, heart rate recovery, skin conductance, and sleep quality changes post-exercise
- Challenges remain in standardizing measurement methods across different wearable devices and brands
- Optimal exercise parameters (intensity, duration, frequency) for mental health benefits are still not well-defined via wearable data
- The review calls for identifying more reliable biomarkers to assess exercise's impact on mental health in future research

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's activity tracking and trend analysis features. ## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Included 90 articles published between 2005 and 2024. Published in Sports Medicine and Health Science (2025). Focused on studies using wearable devices to measure physiological responses during or after exercise in relation to mental health outcomes.
