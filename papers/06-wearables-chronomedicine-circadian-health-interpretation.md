## Summary

This article examines the use of wearable technologies in chronomedicine, focusing on applications in sleep research, circadian medicine, and health monitoring beyond the circadian domain. It emphasizes actigraphy as a validated tool for monitoring sleep, activity, and light exposure, and discusses how wearable-derived circadian biomarkers can inform clinical decision-making.

## Key Findings

- Actigraphy remains a well-validated tool for long-term circadian rhythm assessment in free-living conditions
- Wearable-derived circadian biomarkers (rest-activity rhythm, interdaily stability, intradaily variability) provide clinically meaningful indicators of circadian health
- Multi-modal wearable sensing (combining accelerometry, photoplethysmography, temperature, and light sensors) improves the precision of circadian phase estimation
- Chronomedicine applications include optimizing medication timing, identifying circadian disruption in shift workers, and monitoring treatment response in mood disorders
- Consumer wearables are increasingly capable of providing research-grade circadian data, though standardization of metrics and reporting remains a challenge
- The interpretation of wearable circadian data requires context-aware algorithms that account for individual baselines and environmental factors
