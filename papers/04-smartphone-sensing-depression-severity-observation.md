## Summary

An observation study published in JMIR (2025) investigating various smartphone sensing features, including screen activity, app usage, call behavior, and GPS-based location data, for predicting depression severity. The study evaluates how well passive sensing from standard smartphone sensors correlates with clinically measured depressive symptoms, combining smart sensing with ecological momentary assessment (EMA) data.

## Key Findings

- GPS-based features such as total distance, number of significant places, and time spent at home correlate with depression severity (distance: r=-0.25, time home: r=0.10, normalized entropy: r=-0.17)
- Screen time and app usage patterns provide complementary behavioral markers for depression
- Combining smart sensing with EMA data improves depression severity prediction beyond either method alone
- Location routine index and circadian movement regularity emerged as relevant features for depression assessment
- The study addresses the gap between controlled research settings and real-world deployment of smartphone-based mental health monitoring

## MAON Applicability

Directly supports MAON's multi-signal approach to mental health monitoring, particularly the combination of screen time tracking, location visits, and health data in the cognitive score. The GPS-based features (time at home, location entropy, routine regularity) validate MAON's location domain in the cognitive score, which tracks visit patterns and routine measurement. The finding that combined passive sensing and self-report outperforms either alone supports MAON's integration of physiological data (Apple Health, Garmin) with behavioral signals (screen time, calendar, location) in a single composite score.

## Methodology Notes

Observational study using smartphone passive sensing data combined with EMA. Depression severity measured using validated clinical instruments. Analysis included meta-analytic correlation estimates for GPS features across multiple studies. Published in JMIR's 2025 first issue. The study builds on prior work establishing correlations between digital phenotyping features and mental health outcomes.
