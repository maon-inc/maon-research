## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis evaluates the real-world accuracy of consumer wearable activity trackers for detecting medical conditions and health events. Drawing from 28 studies encompassing over 1.2 million participants, the review covers conditions including COVID-19, cardiac arrhythmias, and falls, with subgroup analyses by device type (Fitbit, Oura Ring, and mixed devices).

## Key Findings

- 28 studies were included with 1,226,801 total participants (ages 28.6 to 78.3 years)
- COVID-19 detection was the most studied condition, with devices showing good sensitivity and specificity
- Atrial fibrillation detection showed the most promising results, with high sensitivity and specificity across devices
- Fall detection demonstrated moderate sensitivity but lower specificity
- Fitbit and Oura Ring were the most commonly studied consumer devices
- Real-world (free-living) performance was generally lower than laboratory performance but still clinically useful
- The pooled area under the curve, accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and positive predictive value were all assessed across conditions

## MAON Applicability

Strongly relevant to MAON's wearable-based illness and anomaly detection features. The meta-analytic evidence supports using consumer wearable data (from devices like Apple Watch, Fitbit, and Oura) for detecting health anomalies in ambulatory settings. The condition-specific accuracy metrics can inform MAON's alert thresholds and confidence levels for health notifications. The finding that real-world performance differs from laboratory settings is important for calibrating MAON's detection algorithms.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Searched 10 electronic databases for studies published through April 2023. Inclusion criteria required use of consumer wearable activity trackers in free-living (non-laboratory) conditions in adults. Risk of bias assessed using QUADAS-2. Subgroup analyses by device type and medical condition. Large aggregate sample but heterogeneity across studies in detection methods and populations.
