## Summary

A scoping review examining how physiological sensors in wearable devices can be used to manage persisting symptoms of Long COVID. The review identified seven types of sensors used across multiple studies, including PPG, ECG, accelerometers, temperature sensors, and others, monitoring metrics such as heart rate, HRV, SpO2, respiratory rate, temperature, physical activity, and sleep.

## Key Findings

- Seven different sensor types were identified: photoplethysmography (PPG), electrocardiography (ECG), accelerometers, temperature sensors, gyroscopes, near-infrared spectroscopy, and electromyography.
- Key physiological metrics monitored include heart rate, HRV, SpO2 (peripheral capillary oxygen saturation), respiratory rate, temperature, physical activity, and sleep-related movements.
- Ten different wearable devices were used across studies for detecting Long COVID symptoms, including commercially available smartwatches and fitness trackers.
- Persistent physiological changes were detectable in approximately 9.4% of COVID-19 positive individuals, most commonly elevated nightly heart rate and reduced HRV metrics.
- Lowered SpO2 combined with increased body temperature during acute infection predicted chronic fatigue syndrome and affective symptoms in Long COVID with 60.7% variance explained.
- Wearable continuous monitoring provides advantages over periodic clinical assessments for tracking symptom fluctuation in Long COVID patients.

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's continuous physiological monitoring through Apple Health and Garmin integration. MAON already tracks heart rate, HRV, and sleep patterns, which are the same signals used for Long COVID detection. The finding that persistent nightly HR elevation and HRV reduction can indicate ongoing illness supports MAON's trend analysis features. SpO2 monitoring, available on Apple Watch, could be incorporated as an additional health signal. This validates MAON's role in detecting physiological anomalies that may indicate illness or recovery status.

## Methodology Notes

Scoping review following PRISMA-ScR guidelines. Published 2025 in the Journal of Medical Internet Research. Reviewed studies using wearable devices for Long COVID symptom management. Included both commercially available consumer devices and research-specific medical devices.
