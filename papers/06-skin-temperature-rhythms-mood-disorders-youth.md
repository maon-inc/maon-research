## Summary

Examines 24-hour wrist skin temperature rhythms in youth with emerging mood disorders compared to healthy controls. Found that mood disorder patients show delayed, less stable, and more variable skin temperature rhythms, with the circadian-bipolar spectrum subtype showing the most pronounced dysfunction.

## Key Findings

- Youth with mood disorders had lower rhythm-adjusted mean skin temperature and delayed peak timing vs controls
- Reduced inter-daily stability and increased intra-daily variability in temperature rhythms in mood disorder group
- The circadian-bipolar spectrum subtype showed the lowest relative amplitude, indicating weakened circadian pacemaker
- Temperature rhythm metrics differentiated clinical subtypes of mood disorders
- Highlights 24-hour skin temperature as a non-invasive wearable biomarker of circadian disturbance

## MAON Applicability

MAON collects wrist temperature nightly via Apple Watch. This paper validates that temperature rhythm disruption is a meaningful marker of mood disorder severity, especially for bipolar-spectrum users. MAON could track temperature rhythm stability over time as a circadian health metric.

## Methodology Notes

Published in Journal of Biological Rhythms (2025). Used wearable skin temperature sensors continuously. Compared healthy controls to youth with mood disorder subtypes. Cosinor analysis and non-parametric circadian rhythm metrics.
