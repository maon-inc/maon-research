## Summary

This CHI 2024 paper presents a large-scale, longitudinal investigation of design frictions as interventions for smartphone overuse, using the "one sec" app that introduces a brief pause before target apps open. The study collected usage data from 1,039 users over an average of 13.4 weeks and qualitative insights from 249 users via an online survey, making it one of the largest and longest real-world studies of digital self-control tools.

## Key Findings

- Short design frictions effectively reduce how often users attempt to open target apps over the long term
- Users demonstrate more intentional app-opening behavior over time, suggesting lasting behavioral change rather than mere friction fatigue
- Users take periodic breaks from the intervention and quickly rebound from overuse patterns when returning
- The brief pause intervention works by creating a moment of reflection, disrupting automatic app-opening habits
- Long-term effectiveness persists over 13+ weeks, unlike many digital wellbeing interventions that show declining effects
- Qualitative data reveals users appreciate the non-punitive, friction-based approach compared to hard blocking

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's app blocking and screen time management features. MAON implements both hard blocking (during calendar events) and threshold-based monitoring with category tracking. This study provides evidence that brief friction-based interventions can be more sustainable than hard blocks alone, supporting a layered approach where MAON could combine its current hard blocking with gentler friction-based prompts outside of blocked periods. The longitudinal sustainability findings (13+ weeks) address a key concern about whether digital wellbeing interventions maintain effectiveness over time. The finding that users take periodic breaks and recover suggests MAON should accommodate flexible blocking patterns rather than demanding strict adherence.

## Methodology Notes

Mixed-methods study published at ACM CHI 2024 (top-tier HCI venue). The large sample (n=1,039) and extended observation period (average 13.4 weeks) provide exceptional ecological validity compared to most digital wellbeing studies. Qualitative component (n=249) adds depth to the quantitative usage data. Limitation is the self-selected user sample (people who downloaded "one sec" are already motivated to reduce screen time), which may overestimate effect sizes for the general population.
