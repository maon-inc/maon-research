## Summary

A study examining 24-hour wrist skin temperature rhythms in young people with emerging mood disorders found that affected youth had delayed, less stable, and more variable temperature rhythms compared to healthy controls. Skin temperature circadian features differed across mood disorder subtypes and clinical stages, suggesting wearable-derived circadian biomarkers can characterize illness trajectories noninvasively.

## Key Findings

- Youth with mood disorders showed delayed peak timing, reduced inter-daily stability, and increased intra-daily variability in skin temperature rhythms
- Participants with mood disorders had lower rhythm-adjusted mean skin temperature compared to controls
- The "circadian-bipolar spectrum" subtype showed lower relative amplitude without sleep midpoint delay
- Different mood disorder subtypes exhibited distinct circadian signatures, supporting biological subtyping
- 24-hour skin temperature rhythms serve as a noninvasive biomarker of circadian disturbance
- Wrist-worn temperature sensors can feasibly capture these rhythm patterns in ambulatory settings

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional study comparing youth with emerging mood disorders to healthy controls using wrist-worn skin temperature sensors over 24 hours. Published in the Journal of Biological Rhythms in 2025. Analyses included cosinor rhythm modeling, inter-daily stability, intra-daily variability, and relative amplitude. Builds on the same research group's earlier work on skin temperature rhythms and mood disorders.
