## Summary

A comprehensive review of wearable technology applications in circadian rhythm research, covering the evolution from traditional actigraphy to modern multi-sensor consumer devices. The review examines how wearables capture physiological parameters including movement, heart rate, skin temperature, and light exposure to analyze circadian rhythms in real-world settings, and discusses both the promise and current limitations of clinical translation.

## Key Findings

- Wearable devices can continuously monitor movement, heart rate, skin temperature, and light exposure, enabling real-world circadian rhythm analysis beyond controlled lab settings
- Published studies on wearable devices increased from 524 in 2014 to 4,215 in 2024, with wearable-circadian research growing from 1 study to 34 studies per year in the same period
- Despite growth in research publications, clinical studies using wearables for circadian monitoring average only about one per year, indicating a significant research-to-practice gap
- Circadian rhythm disruptions measured by wearables are associated with increased risks of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and neurodegenerative disorders
- Key challenges include limited validation against gold-standard methods (dim light melatonin onset), restricted access to raw data from consumer devices, and lack of algorithm transparency
- Multi-sensor approaches combining accelerometry, photoplethysmography, and temperature sensing offer the most promising path toward accurate ambulatory circadian phase estimation
