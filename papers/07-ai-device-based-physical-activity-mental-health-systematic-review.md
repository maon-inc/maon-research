## Summary

A systematic review of 49 studies (from 11,057 search results) examined how AI and machine learning are applied to wearable-measured physical activity data for detecting, predicting, or classifying mental health states. Most studies focused on stress (31%) or depression (29%), with step count and raw accelerometer values being the most common movement measurements.

## Key Findings

- Of 49 included studies, stress (n=15) and depression (n=14) were the most studied conditions
- Step count was the most common movement feature (55% of studies), followed by raw accelerometer values (44%)
- 71% of studies had fewer than 100 participants, and 47% had fewer than 14 days of data
- Studies using multimodal sensor data (combining activity with HRV, sleep, etc.) generally achieved better classification performance
- The field lacks standardized feature extraction and model validation approaches
- Raw accelerometer data provides richer features than summarized metrics like step counts alone

## MAON Applicability

Supports MAON's multi-signal approach to wellness scoring. The review confirms that combining physical activity data with other physiological signals (as MAON does with steps, HRV, sleep, and resting HR in the health domain) improves mental health classification accuracy. The finding that step count alone is limited supports MAON's composite scoring approach. Highlights the value of continuous wearable monitoring for detecting stress and depression, which MAON facilitates via Apple Health and Garmin integrations.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines. Searched PubMed, Scopus, IEEE Xplore, and Web of Science databases for studies published between 2016 and 2023. Included 49 papers meeting criteria of using wearable-measured physical activity in ML pipelines for mental health outcomes. Quality assessment noted significant methodological heterogeneity and small sample sizes as limitations across the field.
