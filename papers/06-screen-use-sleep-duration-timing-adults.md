## Summary

A large cross-sectional analysis of 122,058 participants from the American Cancer Society Cancer Prevention Study-3 examining the association between electronic screen use before bed and sleep outcomes. The study found that daily bedtime screen use was associated with shorter sleep duration, later bedtimes, and worse sleep quality, with effects more pronounced in individuals with a later chronotype.

## Key Findings

- Daily screen use before bed was reported by 41.2% of participants (50,289 of 122,058)
- Daily bedtime screen use was associated with 7.64 fewer minutes of sleep per night (approximately 50 minutes less per week)
- Screen use was associated with a 33% higher prevalence of poor self-reported sleep quality
- Effects were more pronounced in participants with a later chronotype
- Later bedtimes were a key mediating factor between screen use and reduced sleep duration
- The dose-response relationship suggests that even moderate screen use before bed has measurable sleep impacts

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's core value proposition of monitoring screen time and its impact on sleep. The app tracks both screen time (via FamilyControls) and sleep (via Apple Health), and the proactive agent can identify cross-domain patterns like screen time affecting sleep. This paper provides direct evidence supporting the app's approach of monitoring screen use and nagging users about bedtime phone habits. The chronotype finding could inform personalized intervention timing.

## Methodology Notes

Cross-sectional analysis of 122,058 adults (mean age 55.1 years, 69.5% female) from the CPS-3 cohort. Self-reported screen use and sleep measures. Large sample size provides strong statistical power. Published in JAMA Network Open. Limitation: cross-sectional design prevents causal inference; self-reported measures.
