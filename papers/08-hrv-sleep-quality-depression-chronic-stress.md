## Summary

Examines how HRV, sleep quality, and depression interact in the context of chronic stress. Finds that lower HRV serves as a biomarker of sleep reactivity to stress, which increases depression vulnerability.

## Key Findings

- Lower HRV is a biomarker of increased sleep reactivity to stress
- Greater sleep-stress vulnerability increases risk for depressive symptoms during chronic stress
- HRV, sleep quality, and depression form an interconnected triad
- Autonomic, cardiovascular, and respiratory systems all influence HRV
- The parasympathetic nervous system mediates short-term HRV changes relevant to stress response

## MAON Applicability

Directly supports MAON's multi-signal approach: HRV + sleep quality together provide stronger depression risk prediction than either alone, especially during periods of elevated stress. MAON should look for the specific pattern of declining HRV + worsening sleep + elevated stress as a high-risk compound signal.
