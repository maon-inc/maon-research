## Summary

Randomized within-subject crossover trial (n=163 young adults, 1,508 daily observations) evaluating a multifaceted nudge-based intervention to reduce smartphone use. The intervention incorporated subtle changes to phone settings including disabling face/touch ID, setting longer passwords, switching to grayscale mode, and removing social media apps from the home screen.

## Key Findings

- The nudge intervention significantly reduced both screen time and phone checking frequency compared to control conditions
- Participants reported higher stress levels during the intervention period, potentially due to frustration from partial restrictions without full digital disengagement
- The crossover design allowed each participant to serve as their own control, strengthening causal inference
- Combining multiple nudge strategies (friction-based, visual, and environmental) was more effective than single-strategy approaches documented in prior literature
- Effects on checking frequency were more consistent than effects on total screen time, suggesting nudges better reduce habitual phone pickups than sustained usage sessions
- The stress increase during intervention highlights a trade-off between reducing smartphone use and short-term psychological discomfort

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's smart schedule feature, which uses AI-driven app blocking to reduce screen time during specified windows. The finding that nudge-based restrictions reduce checking frequency but may temporarily increase stress informs how MAON should design its blocking experience, potentially incorporating breathing exercises or other stress-reduction tools during active blocking periods. The crossover design provides strong evidence that the mechanism works at the individual level.

## Methodology Notes

Randomized within-subject crossover trial with counterbalancing over a two-week period. Used daily diary methodology paired with objective smartphone usage data. Multilevel modeling accounted for nested daily observations within participants. Limitations include young adult sample (mostly university students) and relatively short intervention period.
