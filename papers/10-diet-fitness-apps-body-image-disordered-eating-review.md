## Summary

A systematic review of 38 quantitative and qualitative studies examining the association between diet and fitness monitoring app use, body image concerns, and disordered eating symptomology. Found that disordered eating was higher in young adults who use these apps and in those who use them more frequently, with qualitative data revealing unintended consequences like pressure to meet goals and guilt.

## Key Findings

- Disordered eating symptomology was higher in diet/fitness app users compared to non-users
- More frequent app use was associated with greater disordered eating symptoms
- Cross-sectional evidence links app use to body image concerns and compulsive exercise
- Qualitative research identified unintended consequences: pressure to meet goals, guilt, and obsessive tracking behaviors
- No causal conclusions could be drawn from the available evidence (mostly cross-sectional)
- The relationship between tracking technology and disordered eating may be bidirectional

## MAON Applicability

Critical safety consideration for MAON's design. As a wellness app that tracks health metrics (activity, sleep, HRV, screen time), MAON must be mindful that presenting physiological data and activity metrics could inadvertently reinforce disordered eating or compulsive exercise behaviors in vulnerable users. MAON's AI agent should be designed to detect and avoid reinforcing obsessive tracking patterns, and the app's framing should emphasize holistic wellness rather than metric optimization.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review of 38 studies (quantitative and qualitative) published in Body Image (2024). Searched multiple databases. Included studies examining diet and fitness monitoring apps in relation to body image and disordered eating. Most studies were cross-sectional, limiting causal inference.
