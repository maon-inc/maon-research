## Summary

A systematic review published in Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback (2025) evaluating the feasibility and effectiveness of portable and remote HRV biofeedback training (P/R-HRVBt) for reducing stress and subclinical anxiety and depression. This review specifically examines interventions delivered outside of clinical settings using consumer-grade wearable devices and smartphone apps, reflecting the shift from laboratory-based biofeedback to accessible, everyday-use approaches.

## Key Findings

- Portable/remote HRV biofeedback training is feasible for home-based and everyday use with consumer wearable devices
- Evidence supports effectiveness for reducing subjective stress and improving HRV metrics
- App-based delivery of HRV biofeedback shows promise as a scalable intervention for subclinical anxiety and depression
- Adherence rates vary across studies but are generally acceptable when the technology is user-friendly
- Wearable-based HRV measurement (wrist-worn devices) provides sufficient accuracy for biofeedback training in non-clinical populations
- The transition from clinical to remote delivery maintains therapeutic benefit while improving accessibility

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's breathing activity feature, which guides users through slow-paced breathing exercises. This review validates the approach of delivering HRV biofeedback remotely via consumer devices (Apple Watch, Garmin) rather than requiring clinical-grade equipment. The feasibility findings support MAON's design of brief, self-guided breathing sessions that users complete independently. The evidence that wrist-worn HRV measurement is sufficient for biofeedback justifies MAON's reliance on Apple Health and Garmin HRV data to inform intervention timing and effectiveness assessment.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following structured search methodology across biomedical and psychology databases. Focused specifically on portable/remote delivery modalities (excluding laboratory-only studies). Assessed both feasibility outcomes (adherence, user experience, technical reliability) and clinical effectiveness outcomes (stress, anxiety, depression, HRV changes). Published in a specialized biofeedback journal, ensuring domain expertise in peer review.
