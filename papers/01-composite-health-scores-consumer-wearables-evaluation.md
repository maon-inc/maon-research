## Summary

A systematic evaluation of composite health scores (CHS) from leading consumer wearable manufacturers, assessing their underlying methodologies, biometric contributors, and scientific basis. The study catalogs CHS features across devices from Fitbit, Garmin, Oura, WHOOP, Polar, Samsung, Suunto, and Ultrahuman, revealing significant variability in how these scores are constructed and a general lack of peer-reviewed validation.

## Key Findings

- Heart rate variability (86%), resting heart rate (79%), physical activity (71%), and sleep duration (71%) are the most frequently incorporated biometric contributors in composite health scores
- Significant discrepancies exist in data collection timeframes, metric weighting, and proprietary scoring methodologies across manufacturers
- No manufacturer fully discloses their exact algorithmic formulas, and few provide empirical validation or peer-reviewed evidence supporting accuracy
- The dominance of HRV and resting HR across CHS reflects their physiological importance for autonomic tone and parasympathetic modulation
- Composite scores aim to simplify complex multi-signal data for consumers, but lack of transparency, population-level validation, and standardized methods limits reliability
- The study provides a systematic framework for evaluating and comparing CHS features that can guide future research and product development

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's cognitive score feature, which combines health data (steps, sleep, resting HR, HRV), screen time, calendar density, and location into a composite wellness score. This paper provides critical context for understanding how other consumer wearables approach composite scoring and highlights the importance of transparency and validation. MAON's open approach to domain weighting and multi-signal fusion can be informed by the gaps this paper identifies in existing commercial implementations.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic evaluation of CHS features from major consumer wearable manufacturers. Analyzed publicly available documentation, white papers, and peer-reviewed literature associated with each manufacturer's composite scoring approach. The study follows a structured comparison framework rather than a clinical trial design.
