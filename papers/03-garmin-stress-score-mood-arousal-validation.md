## Summary

This study investigated what the Garmin Stress Score actually measures by examining its relationship to different mood states. The researchers found that the Garmin Stress Score (GSS) is associated with high-arousal positive mood (e.g., enthusiasm, excitement) but not with negative mood or psychological stress per se. The findings suggest the GSS reflects autonomic arousal levels rather than stress specifically, and the label "Stress Score" may be misleading to consumers.

## Key Findings

- The Garmin Stress Score was associated with high- and moderate-intensity positive mood but not with negative or low-intensity mood states.
- The moods "calm" and "relaxed" showed a negative dose-response relationship with average Garmin Stress Score, confirming that lower HRV-derived scores map to low-arousal states.
- A positive trend was found for "enthusiastic," indicating the score captures sympathetic arousal regardless of emotional valence (positive or negative).
- The researchers concluded that the term "Stress Score" is misleading because the score does not specifically capture negative mood or psychological stress.
- The GSS reliably tracks autonomic activation but does not distinguish between stress-related and non-stress-related arousal.
- These findings have important implications for how consumer wearable "stress" metrics should be interpreted and communicated to users.

## MAON Applicability

Critical for MAON's Garmin integration and how stress data from Garmin is presented to users. The finding that the Garmin Stress Score reflects arousal rather than stress has direct implications for MAON's cognitive score calculations and user-facing messaging. MAON should avoid labeling Garmin-derived metrics as "stress" and instead frame them as "arousal" or "autonomic activation" levels. This research also informs how MAON interprets Garmin stress data in the context of the broader cognitive score, where high arousal may not always indicate a negative state.

## Methodology Notes

Published in the Journal of Affective Disorders Reports, volume 21, pages 1-11 (2025). Used the Garmin Vivosmart wearable combined with ecological momentary assessment (EMA) to capture real-time mood states. Examined dose-response relationships between the proprietary stress score and validated mood dimensions including arousal and valence.
