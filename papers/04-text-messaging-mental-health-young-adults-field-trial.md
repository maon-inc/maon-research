## Summary

A field trial evaluating a text messaging intervention designed to support mental health self-management in young adults. The intervention delivers evidence-based strategies from cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT), and positive psychology through interactive SMS dialogues, with 1- and 2-week versions tested using a Wizard-of-Oz methodology.

## Key Findings

- Young adults showed high levels of engagement with the interactive text messaging program for mental health self-management
- Participants reported that prompt dialogues helped them learn new coping strategies they could apply in daily life
- The text messaging format overcame common barriers to engagement and attrition by reaching users through a communication channel they already use regularly
- Interactive dialogue-based messaging (rather than one-way push notifications) drove higher engagement and perceived value
- Combining strategies from multiple therapeutic modalities (CBT, ACT, positive psychology) provided novelty and variety that sustained user interest
- The Wizard-of-Oz approach validated the conversational design before full automation, providing insights for AI-driven implementations

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's iMessage-based conversational agent delivered through the Sendblue integration. The study validates the core design pattern MAON uses: delivering mental health support through text messaging on users' existing messaging platforms rather than requiring a separate app. The finding that interactive dialogues outperform one-way notifications supports MAON's conversational agent approach. The multi-modality therapeutic strategy (CBT + ACT + positive psychology) could inform MAON's AI agent prompt design for proactive outreach.

## Methodology Notes

Field trial with Wizard-of-Oz methodology where research staff manually sent scripted messages to simulate an automated system. Tested 1-week and 2-week program versions. Qualitative feedback collected through semi-structured interviews. The study focused on engagement and user experience rather than clinical outcomes; a larger RCT would be needed to assess efficacy.
