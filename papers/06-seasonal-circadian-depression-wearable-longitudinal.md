## Summary

A large-scale longitudinal study analyzing 10,018 PHQ-8 depression assessments alongside Fitbit data from 543 participants over up to 2 years. The study extracted 12 circadian rhythm features from 14-day Fitbit windows (sleep, step count, heart rate) and found that higher depression scores were associated with reduced daily steps, increased sleep variability, and delayed circadian rhythms, with significant seasonal modulation of these associations.

## Key Findings

- Higher PHQ-8 depression scores were associated with reduced daily steps, increased sleep variability, and delayed circadian rhythms after adjusting for seasonal effects
- The negative association between depression and daily steps was more pronounced in spring and summer compared with winter, indicating important seasonal modulation
- Twelve circadian rhythm features were extracted from Fitbit data, including cosinor variables (HR acrophase) and nonparametric features (M10 onset of most active 10-hour period)
- Seasonal effects significantly influenced circadian rhythm metrics, underscoring the need to control for time-of-year in mHealth depression monitoring
- Wearable-measured circadian rhythms showed potential as digital biomarkers for depression, particularly when combined across multiple sensing modalities (sleep, activity, heart rate)
- The longitudinal design (up to 2 years of data) strengthens the evidence for using wearable circadian features in real-world depression monitoring

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's integration of sleep, activity, and heart rate data from Apple Health and Garmin. The study's finding that circadian rhythm disruption (measured through the same types of data MAON collects) predicts depression severity supports MAON's approach to multi-signal wellness tracking. The seasonal modulation finding is particularly important for MAON's trends feature, suggesting that wellness thresholds and alerts should account for time-of-year. The circadian features described could inform future enhancements to MAON's cognitive scoring algorithm.

## Methodology Notes

Retrospective longitudinal analysis of 543 participants (76.2% female; median age 48, IQR 32-58 years) from a large mHealth study. Depression assessed biweekly via PHQ-8. Circadian features extracted from 14-day Fitbit windows preceding each assessment. Data span up to 2 years, yielding 10,018 PHQ-8 records. Published in Journal of Medical Internet Research (2024).
