## Summary

Feasibility study evaluating wrist-worn biosensor detection of OCD episodes in adolescents in real-world settings. The "Wrist Angel" device captured physiological signals (pulse, electrodermal activity, skin temperature, acceleration) to predict OCD-related distress events logged by participants.

## Key Findings

- 18 participants (9 OCD patients, 9 controls) aged 8-16 wore biosensors for up to 8 weeks in everyday life
- OCD episodes produced detectable physiological signatures via wrist-worn sensors
- Features extracted from 4 physiological signals (heart rate, EDA, temperature, accelerometry) within sliding windows
- Participants logged OCD distress events via button press on the device
- Pilot results suggest feasibility of detecting OCD episodes from wearable physiological data
- Machine learning could classify compulsive hand washing from routine with 84% sensitivity

## MAON Applicability

MAON tracks HR, HRV, and activity patterns that overlap with the physiological signals used here. For users who self-report OCD, MAON could monitor for physiological arousal patterns consistent with OCD episodes, though the specific EDA signal is not available from Apple Watch.

## Methodology Notes

Published in Frontiers in Psychiatry (2023). Feasibility/pilot study with small sample. Real-world data collection over 8 weeks. Used Empatica E4 wristband. Open access.
