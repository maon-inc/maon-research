## Summary

A month-long randomized controlled trial (N=467) asked participants to use the Freedom app to block all mobile internet access (Wi-Fi and data, excluding calls and texts) for two weeks. Blocking mobile internet significantly improved sustained attention, mental health symptoms, and subjective well-being. Effects were large: 71% of participants showed better mental health, and attention improvements were equivalent to reversing about 10 years of age-related cognitive decline.

## Key Findings

- 91% of participants improved on at least one of three outcomes: mental health, well-being, or sustained attention
- 71% reported better mental health after two weeks; the average improvement in depression symptoms exceeded effect sizes reported in many antidepressant medication trials
- Attention spans improved by an amount equivalent to reversing 10 years of age-related cognitive decline
- 73% reported greater subjective well-being and approximately 59% showed better focus
- Behavioral shifts: participants spent more time exercising, socializing in person, and in nature when internet was blocked
- This was the first pre-registered RCT to use a mobile app to experimentally block internet and directly measure cognitive outcomes

## MAON Applicability

This RCT provides the strongest causal evidence to date for MAON's app-blocking feature. MAON uses Apple's Screen Time API to block distracting apps, which is mechanistically equivalent to the Freedom app intervention studied here. The magnitude of mental health and attentional effects (depression improvement exceeding many medications) is a compelling research backing for MAON's app blocking capability. The behavioral substitution findings (more exercise, socializing) also underscore why pairing blocking with MAON's activity and social context tracking is valuable.

## Methodology Notes

Pre-registered, parallel RCT. N=467 participants from the US. Two-week active intervention (internet blocking via Freedom app) vs. two-week control. Authors: Noah Castelo (University of Alberta), Kostadin Kushlev (Georgetown University), Adrian F. Ward (University of Texas at Austin), Michael Esterman (VA Boston / Boston University), and Peter B. Reiner (University of British Columbia). Published February 2025 in PNAS Nexus, volume 4(2):pgaf017.
