## Summary

A qualitative systematic review examining five JITAIs across nine papers targeting mental health conditions (bulimia nervosa, depression, insomnia, maternal prenatal stress). The review evaluates these interventions against Nahum-Shani et al.'s JITAI framework, finding that while core components were generally incorporated, essential elements like adaptivity and receptivity were often missing or only partially evidence-based.

## Key Findings

- From 1,419 screened records, only 9 papers reporting on 5 distinct JITAIs met inclusion criteria, highlighting the nascent state of the field
- Two JITAIs targeted bulimia nervosa, one targeted depression, one targeted insomnia, and one targeted maternal prenatal stress
- Sample sizes ranged from 5 to an expected 264 participants
- Most core JITAI framework components were incorporated, but essential elements (adaptivity and receptivity) were frequently missing
- Adaptivity (tailoring intervention content and timing based on changing user states) was the least well-implemented component
- Receptivity (detecting when users are most likely to engage with and benefit from an intervention) lacked empirical grounding in most studies
- The review calls for stronger integration of real-time physiological and behavioral data to improve JITAI timing and personalization

## MAON Applicability

Highly relevant to MAON's intervention system design. The review's finding that adaptivity and receptivity are the weakest JITAI components directly informs MAON's approach of using HRV-triggered interventions with personalized intensity and duration parameters. MAON's cohort-based variant system (A/B testing intervention durations) and the proactive agent's skip-logic evaluation align with the framework's emphasis on adaptive decision rules. The gap in receptivity research validates MAON's investment in physiological signal monitoring to determine optimal intervention timing.

## Methodology Notes

Qualitative systematic review. Searched nine databases in August 2023. Included protocol and empirical studies self-identifying as JITAIs targeting mental health, published in peer-reviewed English-language journals. Studies were evaluated against the JITAI conceptual framework components: distal outcome, proximal outcome, decision points, intervention options, tailoring variables, and decision rules. Narrative synthesis was used to integrate findings.
