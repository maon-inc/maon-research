## Summary

A systematic review and meta-analysis evaluating the effectiveness of wearable AI systems for detecting obstructive sleep apnea. The study synthesizes evidence from multiple trials comparing wearable-based AI classification against polysomnography, providing pooled accuracy estimates for different sensor modalities and AI architectures used in sleep apnea screening.

## Key Findings

- Wearable AI systems demonstrate promising accuracy for sleep apnea detection compared to gold-standard polysomnography
- PPG (photoplethysmography) and SpO2 signals from wrist-worn devices are the most commonly used modalities for AI-based apnea detection
- Deep learning architectures generally outperform traditional machine learning approaches for apnea event classification
- Single-channel wearable sensors can approximate multi-channel PSG classification for moderate-to-severe OSA
- Performance varies significantly by apnea severity threshold (AHI cutoffs) and population characteristics
- Ambulatory settings introduce additional noise and motion artifacts that reduce detection accuracy compared to laboratory conditions

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's processing of Apple Health sleep and SpO2 data. The meta-analytic evidence supports the feasibility of detecting sleep-disordered breathing from consumer wearable signals that MAON already collects. Could inform future MAON features that flag potential sleep quality issues beyond simple duration tracking, particularly using overnight heart rate and oxygen saturation patterns.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines. Published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research (JMIR) in September 2024. Includes pooled sensitivity and specificity estimates across multiple study designs.
