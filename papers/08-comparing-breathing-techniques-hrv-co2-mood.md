## Summary

A controlled comparison of three popular breathing techniques -- square breathing, 4-7-8 breathing, and 6 breaths-per-minute (bpm) breathing with two different inhale-to-exhale ratios -- measuring their effects on heart rate variability, end-tidal CO2 levels, and mood in 84 college students.

## Key Findings

- Breathing at 6 bpm increased HRV measures significantly more than either square or 4-7-8 breathing, with small to medium effect sizes
- Neither square breathing, 4-7-8 breathing, nor 6 bpm breathing produced meaningful changes in blood pressure or mood in this acute session design
- 6 bpm breathing led to mild over-breathing (reduced CO2), which is a potential downside to monitor
- The 4-7-8 technique, while popularly promoted by psychotherapists, demonstrated less HRV improvement than the 6 bpm protocol
- Two 6 bpm conditions were tested: a 4:6 inhale-to-exhale ratio and a 5:5 ratio, both outperforming the other techniques for HRV
- Results suggest that breathing rate (specifically 6 bpm) may matter more than specific timing patterns (like 4-7-8) for HRV enhancement

## MAON Applicability

Directly relevant to MAON's breathing activity feature. The app offers guided breathing sessions, and this paper provides evidence for which techniques are most effective at modulating HRV. Since MAON tracks HRV via Apple Health and Garmin, the app could recommend 6 bpm breathing over 4-7-8 for users specifically targeting HRV improvement, while acknowledging that 4-7-8 may still be preferred for subjective relaxation.

## Methodology Notes

Controlled study with 84 college students (60% female). Within-subjects design with counterbalanced conditions. HRV measured via ECG during each breathing condition. CO2 measured via capnography. Mood assessed with standardized instruments. Acute (single-session) design limits conclusions about long-term effects.
