## Summary

A comprehensive meta-analysis of polysomnographic research across multiple mental disorders. It quantifies sleep architecture changes in depression, anxiety, PTSD, and other conditions, providing effect sizes for REM latency, deep sleep, and sleep continuity alterations.

## Key Findings

- Major depression showed the most severe sleep continuity, sleep depth, and REM sleep pressure alterations
- Shortened REM latency, increased REM duration, and increased REM density are biological markers of depression
- Affective disorders showed alterations in most sleep variables compared to healthy controls, except slow wave sleep duration
- PTSD and anxiety disorders also showed distinct sleep architecture patterns
- Sleep architecture changes may precede clinical expression of depression

## MAON Applicability

MAON tracks sleep stages nightly. This paper provides the reference framework for interpreting stage-specific changes: increased REM proportion and decreased REM latency should flag potential depression risk, while overall sleep continuity metrics (fragmentation) are relevant across multiple conditions.

## Methodology Notes

Meta-analysis published in Psychological Bulletin (2016). Covers multiple mental disorders with polysomnographic data. Large aggregate sample across included studies. Highly cited foundational reference.
