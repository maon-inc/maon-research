## Summary

A comprehensive review of the evidence linking environmental noise exposure to mental health outcomes. The paper synthesizes epidemiological and mechanistic evidence showing that chronic noise exposure increases susceptibility to depression, anxiety, and behavioral problems. It outlines two primary pathways: a direct pathway through auditory damage and an indirect pathway through annoyance, sleep disturbance, and chronic stress activation.

## Key Findings

- Environmental noise exposure is associated with increased risk of depression, anxiety, suicide, and behavioral problems in children and adolescents
- The indirect pathway operates through chronic activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis
- Sustained HPA activation leads to systemic inflammation that progresses to neuroinflammation
- Neuroinflammation triggers fear and anxiety responses through amygdala sensitization
- Sleep disturbance is a key mediating mechanism between noise exposure and mental health decline
- Traffic noise (road, rail, aircraft) is the most studied source, with WHO identifying it as a major public health concern
- Even moderate noise levels sustained over time can produce measurable mental health effects

## MAON Applicability

MAON's baseline engine tracks both headphone noise exposure and environmental noise levels with 7-day rolling averages. This review provides the mechanistic foundation for why noise monitoring matters for mental wellness. MAON could use sustained elevations in noise exposure as a signal for increased stress risk, especially when combined with changes in HRV (reflecting HPA axis activation) and sleep quality deterioration. The indirect pathway model (noise -> sleep disruption -> HPA activation -> neuroinflammation) maps directly onto multiple data streams MAON already collects.

## Methodology Notes

Narrative review synthesizing epidemiological cohort studies, experimental evidence, and mechanistic research. Covers evidence from major European noise studies (NORAH, HYENA) and WHO Environmental Noise Guidelines. Strength lies in the mechanistic framework connecting noise to mental health through well-characterized biological pathways.
