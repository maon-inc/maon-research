## Summary

A systematic review of 28 studies (2020-2024) examining the use of wearable sensors combined with artificial intelligence for detecting obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). The review covers device types ranging from consumer smartwatches to purpose-built wearable patches and rings, evaluating their AI-driven approaches to screening for sleep apnea outside traditional sleep laboratories.

## Key Findings

- Reviewed 28 studies across four databases spanning 2020-2024, with increasing publication frequency over time
- Wearable devices classified into bracelets, chest bands, adhesive patches, headsets, and rings
- Apple Watch (Series 9-11, Ultra 2) received FDA authorization in September 2024 for sleep apnea notifications using accelerometer data over 30-day periods (sensitivity 66.3%, specificity 98.5%)
- Samsung Galaxy Watch received FDA De Novo authorization in February 2024 for moderate-to-severe OSA screening (sensitivity 82.7%, specificity 91.1%)
- Consumer devices are approved for screening/notification only, not for diagnosis
- Agreement between wearables and polysomnography tends to decrease as sleep apnea severity increases
- AI models using PPG and accelerometer signals show promising classification accuracy but lack large-scale clinical validation

## MAON Applicability

Relevant to MAON's Apple Health integration, as Apple Watch sleep apnea notifications generate data that could flow into MAON's health monitoring. Supports potential future expansion of MAON's sleep analysis beyond duration and staging to include respiratory disturbance indicators. The systematic review helps contextualize the clinical validity of consumer wearable sleep data that MAON processes.

## Methodology Notes

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines, covering 28 studies from four databases. Studies span laboratory and ambulatory settings with various wearable sensor types. Published in Journal of Medical Systems.
