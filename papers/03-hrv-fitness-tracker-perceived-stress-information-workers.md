## Summary

A large-scale in-situ longitudinal study examining the alignment between consumer fitness tracker HRV and self-reported perceived stress in information workers. Finds significant but modest correlations, with the relationship weakening outside laboratory settings.

## Key Findings

- Significant but small relationship between consumer wearable HRV and perceived stress in daily life
- HRV features explained approximately 1% of variance in predicting individual stress responses
- The HRV-stress association is stronger in lab settings than in real-world daily life
- Individual differences in the HRV-stress relationship are substantial
- Personalized models are needed rather than one-size-fits-all thresholds

## MAON Applicability

Critical calibration data for MAON. The modest correlation between HRV and perceived stress means MAON should use HRV as one signal among many rather than relying on it alone. Supports MAON's multi-signal fusion approach and personalized baseline strategy.

## Methodology Notes

Large-scale longitudinal study published in JMIR Human Factors (2022). Information workers (knowledge workers, MAON's likely user base). Uses commercial fitness trackers in real-world settings. High ecological validity.
