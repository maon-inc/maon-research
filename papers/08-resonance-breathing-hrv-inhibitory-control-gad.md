## Summary

This study examined the effects of a brief resonance frequency breathing (RFB) exercise on HRV and inhibitory control in individuals with generalized anxiety disorder. Participants performed slow-paced breathing at their individual resonance frequency and completed cognitive inhibition tasks while HRV was measured. The study provides evidence on the acute physiological effects of structured breathing in a clinical anxiety population.

## Key Findings

- HRV increased significantly during the resonance frequency breathing condition compared to the control condition in participants with GAD.
- Breathing at resonance frequency (typically 4.5 to 7 breaths per minute) produced measurable autonomic shifts even in brief sessions.
- No significant effects of RFB on inhibitory control or worry were observed during the cognitive tasks.
- HRV during the inhibitory control tasks did not differ between conditions, suggesting acute cognitive benefits may require longer intervention periods.
- The study confirms that even brief single-session breathing exercises can alter autonomic function in anxious individuals.
- Results suggest breathing interventions may need to be sustained or repeated for cognitive benefits to emerge alongside physiological changes.

## Methodology Notes

Experimental study with participants meeting criteria for generalized anxiety disorder. Within-subjects design comparing resonance frequency breathing to a control condition. HRV measured via ECG during breathing and cognitive tasks. Published 2025 in Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback.
